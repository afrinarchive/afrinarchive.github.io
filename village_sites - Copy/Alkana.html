Summaries

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Alkana (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) geographical study' by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Alkana (الكانا), Sati Oshaghi (صاتي أوشاغي), Al-Sati (الصاتي) /1357n - 560m/:

  • Alkana (آلكانا): Name of a Kurdish tribe found in Hakkari (Lerkh, p.46). The Arabized name "Sati" (صاتي) has no meaning.
  • A small village located on the western side of a water stream with steep southern decline toward Alkana Valley, its location is level and small in area, with compact houses.

According to the book Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed from Qatma village:

Sati Oshaghi (صاتي اوشاغي): A village in Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Sheikh al-Hadid District (ناحية شيخ الحديد), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the western-central part of the mentioned mountain on a rocky plateau between two streams bordering it east and west and descending south, with thin white soil. It is located 14km northeast of Sheikh al-Hadid town. It is bordered to the north by a high mountain chain and television broadcasting station at the highest mountain peak and Ma'mal Oshaghi village, to the south by a deep stream and high mountain chain and Haj Bilal village, to the west by a mountain chain and Sa'uljik and Omar Oshaghi villages, and to the east by a deep valley separating Khalil Kolko and Sati Oshaghi villages. The number of houses is approximately 55 houses and its age is around 350 years. Its old dwellings are stone and clay with wooden roofs, overlaid by modern cement buildings that spread north and south. It has electricity network and the village drinks from a water network connected to the irrigation network of Sheikh al-Hadid District villages. Previously they used to drink from a spring at the bottom of the valley next to the old ruined mill. It has primary and preparatory schools shared with Khalil Kolko village. The inhabitants work in rain-fed agriculture (olives, vines, figs, walnuts, almonds) alongside sheep and goat herding. It has a modern olive press. It is connected to the district center by a paved road passing from the western and northern sides of the village. The Haj Bilal farm is affiliated with it. This village is considered one of the old villages in the region and neighboring villages.

Village mayor: Mohammed Khayru Jamil (محمد خيرو جميل)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Alkana

Gundê Alkana, a village in the Şiyê district surrounded by mountains, has a history stretching back 800 to 900 years. It was settled by the Sheikhi (Şêxî) tribe approximately seven generations ago and currently consists of around 100 households, though many families have migrated to Aleppo due to economic hardship. The community faced significant disruption during the Sheikh Said rebellion in the early 20th century, an event that caused the villagers to become refugees and is remembered in their oral history through figures like Emerê Hebeş. Traditional life was centered on agriculture, with villagers using a historic, animal-powered millstone (dalîb) over 150 years old, repurposed from an even older olive press, to grind wheat for local dishes like bulgur and the traditional food "Kitoye." The village is notable for its three water springs, and its name is believed to derive from words meaning "bulls" and "spring." A unique cultural practice is dowsing for water using a forked pomegranate stick, a skill passed down from an engineer in Aleppo. The main spring, Kaniya Alkana, is so central to village life that it is featured in local folk songs and dances. The spiritual life of the community also includes a holy shrine, Ziyareta Şêxîdê, located on a village hill where residents make offerings.

B. Summary from Afrin Flo Transcript of Alkana

The village of Alkana is an ancient settlement in the Shiyeh district, with its history dating back to the time of the Pasha. Its name, "Olkana," is derived from "Kaniya Alê," meaning "Spring of the Flag." The village's heritage is marked by several key historical sites. Most prominent was the "Qestel," a large stone house built during the "time of the Germans" that served as a communal "meclis" (council) and had a defensive role, featuring lower towers and "Signs of Hawar" on its roof; it is now in ruins. For generations, the community relied on a communal millstone, the "Dalîb," which is approximately 125 years old and was used by the entire village to make bulgur. In the "Geliyê Gundî" (Village Valley), there were once three or four water mills ("Aş") for grinding grain; the last remaining machinery was installed in 1985 but fell out of use with the advent of motorized mills. The village also shared a communal water well ("Bîr"), dating from the "time of our fathers and grandfathers," which was used for livestock and personal needs but has since dried up. Historically, Alkana has a close relationship with the neighboring village of Xelîl, and both, along with the village of Hec Bîlal, share a single, ancient cemetery. The primary agricultural activity of the village has traditionally been the cultivation of olives.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Alkana

Gundê Alkana, also known as Şeytana, is a village located in the Mabata district of Afrin, near the villages of Habo and Sûriyo. Its cultural heritage is defined by its old ("kevnar") stone houses, which feature traditional architectural elements like arched ceilings ("qenter"), wall niches ("taq") for storage, and fireplaces ("ocax"). Historically, the village possessed its own olive press ("darîbek"), a common feature for villages of that era. The ruins of one ancient house, estimated to be 300 to 500 years old, were reportedly destroyed by an earthquake ("erdhej"). Near these ruins is a village cistern ("sahrînc") with a basin ("kendir") carved from a single piece of stone. A particularly significant historical house was identified as having been built by Armenians, bearing an Armenian inscription with the date 1325 (likely Hijri, c. 1907 AD), though a resident also stated its age as 325 years. The video also captures traditional practices such as picking black mulberries ("tîyê reş") and the multi-stage process of preparing sumac ("Sumaq"), which involves drying the flowers until they turn red and then grinding them for culinary use.
