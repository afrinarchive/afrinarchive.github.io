Summaries

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Be'rava (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin)' Geographic Study:

Be'rava, Ali Bazan - Ali Bazanli /719n - 6km - 390m/:

  • Be'rava (بعرافو): It is believed that the origin of its name comes from Ber ava meaning "beside the water," and the village is located on the shoulder of Afrin River (نهر عفرين). It is also the name of a Kurdish tribal group found in southern Kurdistan (كردستان) and in Damascus (دمشق), including "Ahmad al-Barafi" (أحمد البارافي), the Damascus Kurdish fighter known during the French Mandate.
  • A medium-sized village overlooking westward over Meydankê lake (بحيرة ميدانكي). East of the village there is an archaeological site containing large burials and multiple-room dwellings carved in rock. The waters of Meydankê lake have reached its western borders.

According to the book Afrin... Her River and Green Hills:

Ali Bazan - Be'rafo (علي بازان - بعرافو):

A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد) belonging to Sharran District (ناحية شران), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب), (699 n).

It is a small village located on the western slope of the northeastern part of the mentioned mountain and overlooks westward onto the Afrin River valley (وادي نهر عفرين) in an elevated position above a limestone plateau, covered by clayey limestone soil. It is 4km away from Sharran town in the northwest direction. It is bordered to the north by a slope and course of Afrin River at a distance of meters and Meydankê village (قرية ميدانكي) and Qarqin Small village (قرية قارقين الصغير), to the south by a valley and plain and mountain chain planted with olive and forest trees and Koblik village (قرية كوبه لك), to the east by a slope and deep valley and Qarqin Large village (قرية قارقين كبير) and Jamanli village (قرية جمانلي), and to the west by Afrin River (نهر عفرين) and plain planted with olive trees and Meydankê town (بلدة ميدانكي).

The number of its houses reaches 35 houses and its age is about 500 years. It is one of the old villages adjacent to the river. The region's settlement is very ancient as evidenced by the presence of an archaeological mound west of the village containing large burials and multiple-room dwellings carved in rock. Its old houses are clay-stone with wooden roofs and the modern ones are concrete. Electricity network is available and the village drinks from a water network connected to wells drilled south of Jamanli village (قرية جمانلي). It connects to the sub-district center by a recently paved road and has an elementary school.

The village residents work in olive cultivation and irrigated vegetables on an area of 943 hectares due to its proximity to Afrin River, and they raise sheep and goats. Among its most important families is the Jawish family (عائلة جاويش), who are among the original inhabitants.

Village Mayor: Ali Mohammed Abdo (علي محمد عبدو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Be'rava

The village of Berava (Be'rava), located in the Şera district of Afrin canton, has a history spanning approximately 300 years. Its name, meaning "by the water," reflects its historical proximity to the Afrin River and numerous springs, though its landscape was later altered by the construction of the Maydanki Dam. The village is surrounded by neighbors such as Qerqîna, Meydankê, Çema, and Derwîş. Historically, Berava was a community hub, featuring a local market ("sûq") and a traditional millstone ("delîb") for processing grain. The area also contains significant historical sites, including six caves, such as the "Cave of Mihemed the Sheikh," which were once used as dwellings. The village has a rich cultural and artistic heritage; it was an early educational center for the region, with a formal school established around 1951 that served students from several nearby villages. It is also known for its tradition of "dengbêjî" (bardic singing), producing artists like the late Silêmanê Çûçelê and Mihemed Welîd. This cultural life extends to women's handicrafts, who traditionally created intricate dowry items ("cêz"), including lace and embroidered pieces for occasions like the Newroz holiday, a practice that has since declined with the availability of mass-produced goods.
