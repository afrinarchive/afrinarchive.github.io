I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Memala (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin / Geographic Study:

Memala (مامالي) (الثدي) /1813n - 218h - 3km - 740m/:

  • Memala (مَمالا): A Kurdish tribe found in Malashgerd (ملاذگرد) northern Kurdistan (كردستان) /Lerkh, p.48/. Al-Istakhri (الاصطخري) says: The "Mamaliya" (مماليه) are among the Kurdish tribes in Fars (فارس) /Mosalli, p.455/.
  • Located on the southwestern slope of Bilaliko (بلاليكو) mountain at the Qilçiq (قلچق) or "Kelacik" (Kelacik) or Al-Hisn (الحصن) site /referring to the ruins existing on it/. The village consists of two parts: eastern and western, separated by a valley through which a paved road passes.

According to the book 'عفرين نهرها ورواييها الخضراء' 'Afrin... Its Rivers and Green Hills':

Western Mamanli (مامانلي غربي): A village in Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد) belonging to Rajo (راجو) subdistrict, Afrin (عفرين) region, Aleppo (حلب) governorate.

It is a large village located on the southeastern slope of the calcareous Qilchiq (قلجق) heights where oak and pasture forests are scattered on its slopes. It overlooks east and south onto agricultural land with red fertile soil. It is 3 km north of Rajo (راجو) town. To the north lies a high mountain chain planted with oak trees and Haidar Oba Si (حيدر أوبه سي) village. To the south lies a slope and plain planted with olive trees and Rajo (راجو) town and Haj Khalil (حاج خليل). To the east lies a mountain chain and rough terrain and a deep valley and a paved road heading to Bilaliko (بلاليكو) and Mamanli (مامالي) farm and Hijmanli (حجمانلي) village. To the west lies a valley and Maskanli (ماسكانلي) village. The number of houses is approximately 35, and its age is approximately 300 years according to the statements of one of the elderly residents. Its dwellings are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs and modern concrete ones. It has electricity and an elementary school and an unpaved dirt road and telephone network extended from Rajo (راجو) town. The residents drink from a well belonging to the state. The residents work in rain-fed agriculture (olives, grains, legumes, grapes) on an area of 218 hectares and raise sheep and goats. Some residents work in charcoal production from oak wood, in addition to raising sheep and goats. The mentioned village is beautiful due to its elevated location and the wide plain planted with olive trees in front of it up to Rajo (راجو) town.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Memala

Mamila is an ancient village in the Afrin region, with an oral history suggesting it is approximately 1,000 years old, a timeline estimated by tracing back 10 to 12 generations. The village's origins lie with two nomadic families who initially lived below a mountain before settling the area, with key founding figures being Ma'm, Lawê, Hemze, and Hebîb; the name "Mamila" itself is believed to be derived from "Ma'm û Lawê." The community grew and organized for mutual protection against bandits, known locally as eşqiya. Historically, the village's economy transitioned from pastoralism, raising cattle and sheep, to settled agriculture, a shift aided by laborers from Turkey who helped cultivate the land into olive groves and vineyards. This agricultural development led to the establishment of five traditional olive presses (ma'ser), each owned by a different family, which are said to be around 300 years old. A central social and administrative hub was the historic house of Khalil Ahmed Agha, built over 250 years ago, which served the entire Sheikh tribe (eşîrê Şêxan) as a place for resolving disputes, holding councils, and as a school for morals and literature. The village also contains a sacred shrine, known as Ziyareta Mêremê or Ziyareta Iskender, where a sacred tree was traditionally visited by women who would make offerings in the hope of conceiving children. Following the Rojava Revolution, the people of Mamila established a village council, which in 2014 opened a weekly market to serve local needs and create opportunities, particularly for women.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Memala 2

The village of Mamola, located in the Çiyayê Kurmênc (Kurd Mountains) and consisting of 70 households divided into Mamola Xerbi (Western) and Mamola Şerqi (Eastern) sections, preserves a significant rain-making ritual known as "Boronlêkirin." This tradition is enacted during times of drought when, as one elder described, the "earth and sky are cracked." The ritual involves the entire community—men, women, and children—participating in a procession that moves from house to house to collectively gather bulgur in a white cloth bag. During this procession, the villagers chant for rain, using both the Kurdish phrase "Allah baran, Xwedê baran!" and a specific Arabic prayer that invokes religious figures like "Nebî Taha el-Mumecjed" (the Prophet Muhammad) and "Ebû Zemzem." A key part of the ritual involves the creation of a doll-like effigy and the use of a wooden clapper to announce their arrival at each home. The collected bulgur, along with salt and firewood also gathered from the community, is then used to prepare a large communal meal featuring traditional dishes such as Germonîskê, Kutilk, and Sarma, which is shared by all. These gatherings also serve as an occasion for the preservation of oral traditions, as demonstrated by a villager who sang a portion of the Kurdish national epic, Meme Alan, referencing key figures like Mamo and legendary places such as Cizîra Botan and the Birca Belek.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Memala

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Memala

The video documents a family outing in the Afrin region, which the narrator identifies as a part of Kurdistan consisting of 366 villages. The picnic takes place in the village of Memala, near its tombs, with the neighboring village of Karkê Jorin also visible. A significant portion of the visit focuses on a modern, community-built, solar-powered water station located at the junction of Dêr Mîşê village. This station was constructed as a charitable act to provide free water for travelers and to honor the souls of the village's deceased. The speaker emphasizes the importance of keeping this communal space clean. The area features several named landmarks, including the "darê tûyê Dêr Mîşê" (the mulberry tree of Dêr Mîşê) and a spring known as "bêrê erebo" (the Arab's spring), which serve as traditional gathering places. The narrative also provides insight into local family customs, as the narrator introduces his maternal uncle, Ali (also called Mela), who is known by the nickname "Elîyê Lîstikê" (Ali the Player) and who gave the narrator his name, Sinos. This sense of community is expanded when Uncle Ali offers a blessing not only to the people of Afrin but also to those in Cizire, Qamishli, and Kobani.
