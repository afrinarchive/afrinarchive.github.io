I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Omera (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / عفرين / Geographic Study:

Omera, عمرانلي (عمرانلي), Al-Umriya (العمرية) /935n - 1008h - 4km - 570m/:

  • Omera (أوميرا): A Kurdish proper name derived from "Omar". It is also the name of a clan from the Milli tribe (قبيلة ملّي) of Qara Jah Dagh Mountain (جبل قره جه داغ), and its inhabitants are west of Qirishahir (قيرشهر), /Zaki, J1, p.414/.
  • A medium-sized village located on a plateau crossed by streams flowing west toward the Afrin River (نهر عفرين) valley, the most important of which is Siman valley (وادي سيمان) with its springs and large plane trees existing in its course. The area located southwest of the village has been deforested.

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها ورواببيها الخضراء' 'Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills':

عمرانلي (عمرانلي): A village in Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد) belonging to Sheran district (ناحية شران) in Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo governorate (محافظة حلب), (897 n).

It is a large village located in the northeastern part of Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد) on an elevated limestone ridge crossed by streams flowing west toward the Afrin River (نهر عفرين) valley. It is connected by fertile vegetation and is 4km away from Sheran town (بلدة شران) in the northeastern direction. It is bordered to the north by fertile agricultural plains and mountain chains with rough terrain and cultivated hills including Tilillaq (تليلاق) and Qozli Bikar (قوزلي بيكار) /Ayn al-Joz (عين الجوز)/, and to the south by a deep valley and mountain chains planted with forest trees, olives, and vineyards, and some slopes and Bafliyun village (قرية بافليون). To the east lie valleys and forest trees and mountain chains and valleys and Qastal Jindo village (قرية قسطل جندو) and Arab (عرب) and Yiran (يران), and to the west a deep valley and a water spring from the western side at the bottom of the valley and mountain chains and Jamanli village (قرية جمانلي).

The number of its houses is about 100 houses and its age is about 350 years. Its dwellings are stone-clay with wooden roofs, and the modern ones are of stone and cement extending east and west on both sides of the road passing through the middle of the village. It has an electrical network and drinking water from the water network taken from artesian wells on the southern side of the village. It has an elementary school and a modern mosque in the center of the village. It connects to the district center and the region Sheran (شران) and Afrin City (عفرين) by a paved road connecting its center to several neighboring villages.

Omranli village (قرية عمرانلي) is one of the beautiful villages in the region due to its good location and its elegant and beautiful houses and the trees surrounding it from all directions. Its inhabitants work in olive, vineyard, walnut, pomegranate, and almond cultivation and sheep raising, and it is close to the Afrin River (نهر عفرين) course.

It is mentioned that the artist and buzuq player Mohammed Haj Walo (محمد حج ولو) is one of the sons of this village.

Village mayor: Rashid Walo (رشيد ولو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Omera

Life in the village of Omerê, as recalled by its elders, was a self-sufficient and laborious agrarian existence rooted in the land. The community's life revolved around farming (cotkarî), from plowing and planting to harvesting grain, which was then threshed (şuxrat) and taken to the local mill, specifically identified as the Mill of Hesen Teyar (Aşê Hesen Teyar), to be ground into flour. Women played a central role in the domestic economy, functioning as weavers (terzî) and tailors (xeyat). They used spindles (teşî) to spin wool and were responsible for creating all the family's clothing, including specialized garments like the groom's wedding attire (cilê mîrî). Winter provisions were secured through traditional preservation methods, such as making various products from local grape varieties like Mawnî and Hasirûn. These included molasses (dims), fruit leather (bastiq), and sweets (kêsme), often clarified using a specific white soil (axê kelkê). Other preparations included melon jam made from the local harvest and date-and-nut sweets known as Heşî. Communal life was also evident in daily activities, such as washing clothes at the local spring in the Valley of Simane (Geliyê Sîmanê). Despite the immense physical hardship (cefa) of this past era, which lacked modern amenities, the speaker reflects that life was fundamentally good, underscoring a deep connection to their traditional way of life.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Omera 2

The traditional process of making grape molasses (dems) in the region involved a significant evolution in technology and craft, as remembered by local practitioner Dawûd. Historically, the grape presses (ma'serêd) were constructed from natural materials like white clay (ta'toç) and wood, and the process was a laborious, week-long affair where grapes were stomped by foot—a method now considered unhygienic. Dawûd recalls his father using baskets (şelîf) and a donkey to transport grapes to their family's old press at a specific site named Gûnê Kolî. This tradition was once common in other villages like Dîdonê, near the Turkish border, but has since been replaced by modern, piston-driven metal presses. The craft extends to a deep knowledge of agriculture; the Dûkelgonê kevin grape is prized for producing the best thick, dark molasses, while the white Fransî grape is reserved for syrup. A key step in the process involves using a special white clarifying soil known as hûle or qat'ondin. The final stage involves boiling the juice for nine to twelve hours over a fire fueled by cift (olive pomace), collecting the foam to create a secondary product called solanê, and critically, allowing the molasses to cool completely before tinning to prevent spoilage and rust.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Omera 3

In the village of Omaro, the tradition of tanbur playing is embodied by Mihemed Hajwalo, a 62-year-old musician who has dedicated 35 years to the art. His musical journey began under the influence of his late father, Hajwalo, a respected player who ceased his music after performing the Hajj pilgrimage and passed away in 1988. Mihemed learned by secretly playing his father's instrument, continuing a legacy that was part of a wider musical community in the Afrin region. During his father's time, other notable tanbur players included his cousin in their village, as well as Azadê Meyrê and Menanê Cafo in Shera, Reşîdê Mihemed in Jinderes, and Porxort and Husî Xal in Mabata. For Mihemed, this older style of music represents the authentic core of Kurdish culture, a form of dengbêjî (epic storytelling) that serves as the primary vessel for unwritten Kurdish history. He identifies specific historical epics, such as "Dersimê Xaracho," "Kulka Silêman," "Goraşa Merzel Begê," and the stories of "Adulê û Emer Begê" and "Şerîf Begê," not merely as songs but as the foundational historical records of the Kurdish people. A central theme of his account is a deep regret that this vital oral history has never been systematically recorded or preserved. Despite this, his dedication to the tradition has been recognized, notably in 2009 with an award in Afrin and another from an arts festival in Aleppo, which included a statue named "Ornina," representing an ancient queen and musical figure from Kurdish history.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Omera 4

The village of Omero, located in the Shara district of Afrin, traces its origins to its first settler, a man named Omer from the Shekakan tribe, with kinship ties extending to nearby villages like Durakliya and Shiltah. Historically, the community's livelihood was deeply rooted in pastoral and agricultural traditions; ancestors were nomadic shepherds (koçer) who once managed flocks of over 10,000 sheep, and farming was a laborious, self-sufficient process involving harvesting with sickles, threshing with animals on a "curn," and grinding grain at watermills. Daily life was difficult, lacking modern amenities and requiring villagers to carry water in jugs from local springs. Due to the poor quality of the local land, many residents historically migrated for work to Aleppo, Lebanon, and Turkey. This has evolved into a modern economy based more on local crafts and industries, particularly the production of traditional foods. A central part of this heritage is the processing of grapes—using varieties like Zeynebî and Zehlewî—into a range of products including molasses (dibs), raisins (meyîj), and fruit leather (bastîq). The traditional method for making molasses involves clarifying the grape juice with a special white soil called "axê heware" from the village of Kefer Cenê before boiling it for hours. Other traditional foods include dried figs stuffed with walnuts and sesame, and "Telme," a fresh cheese-like dish made by curdling goat's milk with fig sap. Alongside these culinary traditions, the village has a strong heritage of oral history, preserved and transmitted through the tanbur. As recounted by the musician Mihemed Hec Welo, who comes from a multi-generational family of players, the tanbur was the primary vehicle for narrating Kurdish history, stories, and ancient epics like "Dewrêşê Evdî" and "Mem û Zîn" long before the advent of modern recording technology.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Omera 5

The village of Omero, located in the Shara district, is a community where Kurds and Arabs have historically lived together. The village's name originates from its first settler, a man named Omar from the Mîlî tribe, whose son was named Kêma. The region is predominantly inhabited by the Sheikhan tribe. The Kurdish population of Omero is composed of five related families—the House of Welîkê Laronê, the House of Çomênê, the House of Hewrênê, the House of Qorêşê, and the House of Hecî Huseyn—who all trace their origins to a place called Kevenda or Pînbûg. The Arab population includes three households: two from the Bu Sultan tribe (the houses of Huseyn and Aliwi) and one from the Na'imi tribe (the house of Sheikh Yusuf), who migrated to the village from Kevenda approximately 300 years ago.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Omera

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Omera

The village of Omara (Gundê Omara), located in the Afrin region, holds a rich history that its elders trace back at least 300 to 400 years. According to oral tradition shared by resident Muhammad Haji Walo, the village was named after its original settler, Omar. The area also carries an even more ancient name, Geliyê Şîlo (Shilo Valley), a name of such historical significance that it was said even Alexander the Great could not change it. In the past, Omara was considered a "village of villages," suggesting a central and self-sufficient community where life was deeply connected to the land. This heritage is preserved through traditional paintings, one of which is dated to 1930, depicting rural farming activities, and through the collection of historical artifacts like old tools and lanterns. Beekeeping has been a significant local practice for generations, with some families involved since at least 1986, producing pure, guaranteed honey from the surrounding pastures. In modern times, the village is undergoing reconstruction, with residents clearing rubble from old and damaged houses. The community also reflects the broader trend of emigration, with some of its members now living in Europe. The village cemetery serves as a testament to its long history, containing the graves of its people, such as that of Ahmed Murad, who lived from 1961 to 2019.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Omera

The village of Amara, estimated to be 300-400 years old, derives its name from its founder, a Kurd named Amar who migrated from the plains of Bi Sibil. He established the settlement by building a small room (maq'ad) and a stable (darav), and the location became known as "Amar's Village." The village's foundation is rooted in two primary tribes, the Mûsî and the Bilêlî, from which later tribes like the Reşik and another Amar group emerged; the speaker himself identifies his lineage as Mala Nasûr and Hawar Bilêlê, and can recall seven generations of his ancestors. Near the village stands a cave-like structure called Bana Qewal, which is associated with the Qewal Kurds from the Zardasht region. According to the village elder, life in the past was a thousand times better, characterized by a strong sense of trust (sîqe), security (eman), mutual help (alîkarî), and general abundance (xêr û bêr). This social fabric, where people frequently visited one another, has since been lost, leading to a present-day life that the speaker describes as conflicted and lacking the communal support of the past.
