I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Baliya (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' / 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) geographical study':

Baliya (بالیا), Bali Koy (بالي كوي), Bali (بالي) /1054N - 140ha - 4km - 800m/:

  • Bal (بال): in Kurdish means "the high elevation," which is a description that applies to the village's location. Baliya (بالیا) is also the name of a Kurdish tribe found near Khoy (خوي) in eastern Kurdistan (/A. Zaki, Vol.1, p.390/. The Arabized name "بال" (Bal) has no connection to the old name except linguistically.
  • A small village consisting of two parts: Upper: large, located beside the border, and it is the old one. Lower: small, established after some residents preferred to move down from the rugged slope to facilitate services and stay away from Turkish border guards. The distance between the two villages is about 1km. Its location is strategic on the eastern slope of the "Girê Mezin" (گرێ مەزن) mountain.

According to the book Afrin... Her River and Green Hills:

Bali Koy (بالي كوي): A village in the Afrin (جبل الكرد) belonging to Bulbul District (ناحية بلبل), Afrin Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a very small village meaning "honey village" in the local language. It is located near the current Turkish border in a strategic position on the middle northeastern slope of the limestone "Damarik" (داميريك) mountain covered with green and basalt rocks. Its soil is limestone volcanic rich in water and forests. It is located in the northern section of the Mount Aleppo (جبل حلب) mass, 3km northeast of Bulbul (بلبل) town.

It is bordered to the north by a stream valley and mountain chain and direct Turkish border meters away, to the south by a mountain chain and "Qurnah" (قورنة) village 1km away, to the west by the "Damarik" (داميريك) mountain, the highest peak in the Afrin Region (جبل الكرد) and Aleppo (حلب) with an elevation of 1269m and "Za'reh" (زعره) village, and to the east by a slope and deep stream valley and Turkish border and "Hay Oghli" (هاي أوغلي) and "Mahmoud Oba Si" (محمود أوبه سي) villages.

The village has 25 houses and is about 350 years old. Its dwellings are stone and mud with flat wooden roofs or modern stone and cement houses. It has an electricity network and a clean, cold water spring flowing from the bottom of the high mountain from the western side, considered one of the purest drinking water sources in terms of taste.

The residents work in rainfed agriculture on an area of 130 hectares, producing olives, grains, and vineyards irrigated from the spring and artesian wells on an area of 10 hectares of summer vegetables and fruit trees, in addition to raising sheep, goats, and bees. The village drinks from spring water located east of the village or from cisterns that collect rainwater. It has a border station and is connected to Bulbul (بلبل) town by a paved mountain road 6km long. It is a very close village overlooking the Turkish villages, separated by a very deep stream valley.

Village Head: Hikmet Khalo Mohammed (حكمت خلو محمد)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Baliya

The village of Baliya, situated on the slopes of Bilbilê Hill near the border, holds a history deeply intertwined with the political division of Kurdistan. According to local accounts, the area's history dates back to the Hurrian period, with a castle on the nearby Gir mountain that served as a cool summer retreat for Hurrian royalty. The village's name itself has evolved; it was once known as "Gawir Dehat," a name linked to the agricultural materials like gypsum and fertilizer used in the region. Its current name, "Baliya," is said to be derived from the Turkish word for honey, reflecting a time when the village was famous for its beekeeping. The pivotal event in its modern history occurred in 1938 when the border between Northern and Western Kurdistan was formally established, physically splitting the once-unified village. This division, which involved bombing and the installation of a wire fence, created a 5-kilometer-wide forbidden zone on the villagers' land and separated the community into Baliya Jêrîn (Lower Baliya), which became part of Northern Kurdistan, and Baliya Jorîn (Upper Baliya), which remains in Rojava. The hardships imposed by this border forced a significant migration, with approximately sixty families relocating to the nearby town of Bilbilê. This history of migration also led to the founding of the neighboring village of Alke, established around sixty years ago by people from the Baliya area. More recently, following the start of the Rojava Revolution, the border fortifications were expanded with new military posts, further cementing the division.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Baliya 2

The village of Baliya, located in the Bilbilê district of Afrin canton at the foot of Girê Bilbilê (Bilbilê Hill), possesses a history deeply intertwined with the broader political division of Kurdistan. Historically, the area was significant during the Hurrian period, when a castle on the nearby Gir Mountain served as a summer retreat for kings and emirs due to its cool climate. The village's name is derived from the Turkish word for "honeycomb," a reference to its historical self-sufficiency and the widespread cultivation of plants used for beekeeping. This traditional way of life was catastrophically disrupted in 1938 when the creation of the border between Turkey and Syria physically divided the single village. The northern part, which fell into Bakurê Kurdistanê (Northern Kurdistan), was renamed Jûqoyî, while the southern part remained in Rojavayê Kurdistanê (Western Kurdistan) and is also known today as Gundê Alkê. This division, enforced by a wire fence, rendered up to five kilometers of the villagers' ancestral lands and property inaccessible and resulted in the displacement of 30 to 50 families who resettled in the nearby town of Bilbilê. A Turkish military guard post was subsequently established on the villagers' former lands. Despite this separation and the loss of free movement that had existed for roughly 60 years, the community has preserved its cultural traditions, such as the preparation of ancient foods like keşk (a dried yogurt and grain mixture) and two types of kufte (kibbeh)—one boiled for the sick and another fried for the healthy.
