I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Sêwiya (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد (عفرين)' 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin City)' geographical study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (محمد عبدو علي):

Sêwiya (اوكسوزلي), اليتيمة /798n - 390ha - 12km - 700m/:

  • Sêwiya: The folk name meaning "the orphan" (اليتيم), and this orphan who gave his name to the village has an old and well-known story related to the Umayko family (آل عميكي), the old inhabitants of the village. As for the Ottoman name Uksuzli (أوكسوزلي), and the Arabized al-Yatimah (اليتيمة), they are both translations of the original Kurdish name.
  • A small village located on a high mountain summit, giving it from afar a beautiful and fortified natural view. It has a shrine at its eastern edge, and next to it graves dated 180 years ago.

According to the book: 'عفرين.... نهرها ورواbiها الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills' by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatmeh village (قرية قطمه):

Uksuzli (اوكسوزلي): A village in Kurd Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Mabetli District (ناحية المعبطلي), Afrin City Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the central section of the mentioned mountain, on a rocky tongue forming the western end of Hawari Mountain (جبل هواري). It overlooks a late plain to the west where the railway /Aleppo _ Meydan Ekbas/ (حلب _ ميدان إكبس) extends, and it is to the north of Mabetli town (بلدة معبطلي) at a distance of 12km. Its soil is clay-based. To the north it is bordered by a harsh steep slope and Barbaneh village (قرية بربنده), to the south by a steep harsh slope planted with olive trees and Kul Ibo village (قرية كل إيبو), to the west by a steep harsh slope and al-Tirah valley (وادي الطيرة) and Meydan Ekbas railway (خط سكة حديد ميدان إكبس) and Hamashlak village (قرية حمشلك), and to the east by a slope planted with olive trees and Hayat village (قرية حياة). A deep valley passes through the center of the village dividing the village into two parts: the elevated section in the northwest and the lowered section in the south. It contains approximately 45 houses (بيتاً) and is about 500 years old (عاماً). Its old houses are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, and the modern ones have cement spreading eastward. It has an electricity network and a water network connected to a well south of Kul Ibo village (قرية كل إيبو). It has a primary school and a paved road to the center of the village and neighboring villages. The inhabitants work in rain-fed agriculture on an area of 270ha (grains, legumes, olive trees, and grapevines) and they cultivate irrigated wells on an area of 20ha of summer vegetables alongside raising sheep and goats. Among its most important families is the Umayko or Umako family (عائلة عميكي أو عمكو) (they were the first to inhabit the village since ancient times). There is a modern olive press in the village.

Village headman: Ahmad Zamji Haidar (أحمد زمجي حيدر)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Sêwiya

The village of Sêwiya, located in the Maabatli district of Afrin canton on a high mountain 1000 meters above sea level, has a deep and layered history. Its name is said to derive from the large apples ("sêv") that once grew there. The village's history is marked by a significant event in 1775 when the local leader, Omar Agha (also known as Emîko), refused to pay taxes to the Ottoman state. This act of defiance led to a fierce battle in which Turkish occupiers killed the villagers and exiled many families to regions like Hama, Homs, and Ankara, subsequently repopulating the area with Arabs. Following this, the village was resettled by Kurds from the Şêxiya and Biyan tribes. Historically, the region was part of a larger territory ruled by Ocaqê Îzedîn, a Yazidi leader of the Milan tribal confederation, who resisted Ottoman authority. West of the village lies a site called Bîra Kêrbezê, where a major battle occurred between the Ayyubid army and Crusaders. This battle resulted in the martyrdom of the Ayyubid commander, Hecî Yûsif, and Omar Agha's daughter, Zîzê Xatûn, both of whom are still commemorated in local traditions through tomb visitations and folk songs. The primary occupation for the villagers for over 150 years has been quarrying the local stone, which is sold to surrounding areas for construction. This economic activity is complemented by rich cultural traditions, including the communal preparation of winter provisions like makdous and a unique children's ritual for teething known as "Şeqûlê" or "Kustek," which has been passed down through generations.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Sêwiya

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Sêwiya

In the rural villages of the Afrin region, a food known as Telme is considered a deeply important cultural heritage, described by local shepherd Ahmed of Sêwiyê village as both "traditional and sacred" (turasî û muqedes). This ancestral practice, passed down from the "forefathers" (bav û kal), involves a specific and time-sensitive method. Fresh, warm milk, preferably from a ewe as it is considered the best, is collected in a bowl. The white sap, or latex (şîrê karkê), from a freshly broken fig branch is then squeezed into the milk, which acts as a natural coagulant. After being stirred with a fig twig, the milk sets into a solid consistency within minutes. Traditionally, it is served with grape molasses (dimsê tirî) and eaten with bread. The knowledge of this process is unique to the 366 villages of Afrin and is not practiced in other Kurdish regions like Kobani or Amuda. The tradition is strictly seasonal, viable only during a 40-day window between the 20th and 60th day of spring, as the fig sap loses its coagulating properties after the fig harvest begins. This ancient agricultural cycle, however, is under threat; a recent severe drought has drastically reduced the local harvest season from its traditional two months to a mere ten to twelve days, impacting all aspects of rural life that support such traditions.

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Sêwiya 2

The village of Sewiyê in Afrin is described as having a history stretching back thousands of years, with its name believed to originate from the Kurdish phrase "Sê wîyê" (three of them), referencing a time when it was unified with two other ancient settlements, Kûpka and Sewîdêrê. The village is home to significant historical and cultural sites, including the approximately 1000-year-old shrine of Hecî Yûsif, which served as a major pilgrimage destination for the entire Afrin region, where people would make sacrifices and offerings, especially during the spring festival. The village cemetery is also of historical importance, said to have been established to bury the dead after a battle during a period referred to as the "time of the Kurdish government," and its sanctity is reinforced by a local folk tale about the dire consequences of removing a stone from its grounds. The past way of life was marked by hardship, with villagers undertaking seasonal migrations on foot to neighboring villages like Ba'dîno for the grain and olive harvests. A key cultural tradition that endures to this day is the communal preparation of "Kutayê," a Kurdish dish made from wheat and bulgur that is pounded with large wooden pestles and shared among all the houses during the festival of Ashura.
