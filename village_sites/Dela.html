I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

# Dela (داليانلي)

Villages of Mabetli District (المعبطلي) / Mabeta

Dela (داليانلي) - Dela

===

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' (Afrin City) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Dela (داليانلي), Al-Daliya (الدالية) /431N - 535m/:

  • Dali (دالي): One of the Kurdish Rashwan tribes.
  • A small village located on the southern slope of Khastiya Mountain (جبل خاستيا). Caves and valleys sloping south toward Khastiya valley (وادي خاستيا) abound in and around it. Carved stones and building foundations are observed between the village houses and alleys indicating the existence of ancient settlement dating back to ancient times.

According to the book: 'عفرين.... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills' by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed from Qitma village (قطمه):

Dalyanli (داليانلي): A village in Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Mabetli District (ناحية المعبطلي), Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the central part of the mentioned mountain on the southeastern surface of a limestone plateau extending north and south. Caves abound in it and are crossed by stream valleys toward the southwest, 15km away from Mabetli town (بلدة معبطلي) toward the southwest. Its soil is clay spread over it with pastures and forests.

It is bordered to the north by a high mountain chain planted with forestry trees and Tur Mishkanli village (قرية طور مشكانلي), to the south by a steep slope and the fertile Jarjam valley (وادي جرجم) and Kordanli village (قرية كوردانلي), to the west by a slope and deep stream and Ramdanli village (قرية رمضانلي), and to the east by a high mountain chain and Sili valley (وادي سيلي) and Haj Qasimli village (قرية حاج قاسملي).

The number of houses is about 40, and its age is about 250 years. Its old houses are stone and clay, with flat wooden roofs. Modern concrete construction intermingles with the old in the southwest of the village. It has electricity network and primary school and a paved road to the village center. Currently the village drinks from a state-owned well in Jarjam valley (وادي جرجم).

Its inhabitants depend on cultivating grains, legumes and olives rain-fed, in addition to raising sheep, goats and cattle. Water flows at the bottom of the village from the southeastern side from Rutanli spring (نبع روطانلي) passing through several villages to the deep plain within the lands of Iskenderun district (لواء اسكندرن).

Among the village families is Haj Hamoudi (حج حمودي), and there are university degree holders and cultural personalities (Hussein Bozo (حسين بوزو) / Afrin City singer (زانا عفريني)) and Othman Abdulhanan (عثمان عبدالحنان).

Village mayor: Zahed Ikhtivar (زاهد اختيار)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

The speaker discusses involvement in Kurdish theatre starting in 1989 with the group Koma Nîştiman (04:11-04:18). In 1994, they helped organize the first Kurdish Theatre Festival in Syria, involving groups from Heleb, Cezîrê, and Efrînê (04:33-05:04). A second festival attempt in 1995 failed due to circumstances (05:26-05:38). Traditional storytelling by grandparents in homes and chieftains' rooms was common in the past (05:52-06:13). An effort was made before the Rojava administration to collect endangered traditional folk songs from villages in Çiyayê Kurmênc, documenting 102 songs (10:22-11:00), which led to the Howarê festival (11:07). The village's original inhabitants were reportedly shepherds from Hisê (15:18-15:33). Past traditions included communal henna nights and singing (17:07-17:10), which an elder feels were more proper than current practices (17:30-17:31). Villagers historically sourced water from a spring in Tirmişan (20:00-20:08) and Geliyê Circimê used to have abundant water (20:20-20:29). The craft of making walnut sweets (sincq) is passed down through generations (22:44-22:51). A villager, Hecî Hemûdî, served as an Ottoman officer in Yemen during WWI (1916) (24:12-24:20) and fought alongside Atatürk at Çanakkale in 1915 (24:28-24:32). The local soap-making tradition dates back at least two generations, learned from Armenian refugees from Diyarbekir (25:32-25:53). The village founders were religious teachers (xoceler) from Bilêl Ko who initially lived seasonally in caves near Yajî Aşqar (33:24-33:51, 40:00-40:07). The first permanent settlers were the Mala Hemîdî family (40:21-40:37). The village name Dêla might derive from 'dîn' (religion), referring to its founders (40:48-41:18). The village comprises three main lineages: Mala Hemîdî, Mala Wûskê (including Mala Bûzû), and Mala Çawê (from Qudê) (42:01-42:28). Geliyê Dûlê/Circimê historically had seven operating water mills (42:48-42:53). The area was previously known for vineyards before olive trees became dominant (43:36-43:48) and supplied pomegranate saplings to the Efrîn region (43:55-43:59). Ancestors survived by making charcoal, gathering plants, and herding (45:03-45:15). Yajî Aşqar is an old, now ruined settlement (45:59-46:02). Sarên Cadûr was the traditional water source for drinking, washing, and livestock (46:08-46:19). Geliyê Dûlê/Circimê historically featured seasonal springs flowing until late summer (46:20-46:33).
