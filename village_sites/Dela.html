Summaries

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Dela (English)

Villages of Mabetli District (المعبطلي) / Mabeta

Dela (داليانلي) - Dela

===

According to the book جبل الكرد|Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Dela (داليانلي), Al-Daliya (الدالية) /431N - 535m/:

  • Dali (دالي): One of the Kurdish Rashwan tribes.
  • A small village located on the southern slope of Khastiya Mountain (جبل خاستيا). Caves and valleys sloping south toward Khastiya valley (وادي خاستيا) abound in and around it. Carved stones and building foundations are observed between the village houses and alleys indicating the existence of ancient settlement dating back to ancient times.

According to the book Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed from Qitma village (قطمه):

Dalyanli (داليانلي): A village in Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Ma'batli District (ناحية المعبطلي), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the central part of the mentioned mountain on the southeastern surface of a limestone plateau extending north and south. Caves abound in it and are crossed by stream valleys toward the southwest, 15km away from Mabetli town (بلدة معبطلي) toward the southwest. Its soil is clay spread over it with pastures and forests.

It is bordered to the north by a high mountain chain planted with forestry trees and Tur Mishkanli village (قرية طور مشكانلي), to the south by a steep slope and the fertile Jarjam valley (وادي جرجم) and Kordanli village (قرية كوردانلي), to the west by a slope and deep stream and Ramdanli village (قرية رمضانلي), and to the east by a high mountain chain and Sili valley (وادي سيلي) and Haj Qasimli village (قرية حاج قاسملي).

The number of houses is about 40, and its age is about 250 years. Its old houses are stone and clay, with flat wooden roofs. Modern concrete construction intermingles with the old in the southwest of the village. It has electricity network and primary school and a paved road to the village center. Currently the village drinks from a state-owned well in Jarjam valley (وادي جرجم).

Its inhabitants depend on cultivating grains, legumes and olives rain-fed, in addition to raising sheep, goats and cattle. Water flows at the bottom of the village from the southeastern side from Rutanli spring (نبع روطانلي) passing through several villages to the deep plain within the lands of Iskenderun district (لواء اسكندرن).

Among the village families is Haj Hamoudi (حج حمودي), and there are university degree holders and cultural personalities (Hussein Bozo (حسين بوزو) / Afrin City singer (زانا عفريني)) and Othman Abdulhanan (عثمان عبدالحنان).

Village mayor: Zahed Ikhtivar (زاهد اختيار)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Dela

The village of Dela, located in the Mabeta district of Afrin, is situated in the historically significant Circimê Valley, which residents recall was once filled with water before being transformed into a landscape cultivated with olive, fruit, and especially walnut trees. The village's history traces back to its settlement by three primary families—Mala Hemîdî, Mala Wîskê, and Mala Çawê—after the area was inhabited by nomads, and its name 'Dela' is said to mean 'pious people' (dîndar), reflecting the founders' character. This heritage is preserved through traditional crafts, such as the multi-day communal process of making sincûq, a sweet made by dipping strings of walnuts into a cooked mixture of grape molasses, flour, and starch. Other vital crafts include traditional stonemasonry, practiced by figures like Apê Ehmed who carves tombstones, and the making of olive oil soap, a skill Apê Mihemed has practiced for forty years. Notably, the knowledge for both stonemasonry and soap making was passed down through generations, having originally been learned from Armenian craftsmen, highlighting a history of inter-ethnic skill sharing in the region. This traditional craftsmanship is also reflected in the village's older architecture, which featured thick stone walls for natural insulation, a practice now largely replaced by modern concrete construction. Beyond these crafts, the region has a modern cultural history, exemplified by figures like Mamoste Hisên, who founded the theater group Koma Niştiman in 1989 and was instrumental in organizing the first Kurdish Theater Festivals in Syria.
