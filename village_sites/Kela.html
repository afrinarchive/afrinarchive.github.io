I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Kela (English)

According to the book "Mountain of the Kurds" (جبل الكرد) (Afrin City) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (الدكتور محمد عبدو علي):

Kela, Kalanli, al-Malsa'a (المسلاء) /228n, 560m/:

  • "Kela" (كه لا) is the name of a Kurdish tribe. The Arabic name has no connection to the popular name.
  • A small village located on the southern side of the middle of Kalanli valley (وادي كلانلي). Its location is forest and rugged. There is a spring "Kela" (كلا) K.Kela with sweet water beside the village and near the valley bottom. The village location has a picturesque nature.

According to the book: Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatma village (قرية قطمه):

Kalanli (كلانلي): A farm in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد), belonging to Sheikh al-Hadid District (ناحية شيخ الحديد), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small farm located on an elevation and mountain plateau surrounded by three streams except the eastern one. It is bordered to the north by deep valley and Haj Bilal village (قرية حاج بلال), to the south by high mountain chain and Turmishkanli village (قرية طورمشكانلي), to the west by deep stream and high mountain chain planted with olive and forest trees and Sheikh Jiqallali Foqani and Tahtani village (قرية شيخ جقاللي فوقاني وتحتاني), and to the east by high mountain chain planted with forest trees and Ma'sara Jiq village (قرية معصرة جق).

The number of its houses reaches about 15 houses and its age is 125 years. Its dwellings are stone-clay with wooden roofs. The modern ones are concrete. The inhabitants work in olive, walnut and vine cultivation. It connects with the sub-district and neighboring villages by recently paved road. Electricity network is available since 2006 (2006).

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Kela

The village of Kela, located on a mountaintop in the Shiyeh district, is approximately 200 years old and was established before the surrounding villages. Its name originates from a fortress ("kela") that once stood on the mountain above the main spring, which was used as a hideout by bandits ("çete" or "keleş") who controlled the local roads. The village was founded by Hebîb Birîm of the Aso Zewcî family, who moved there from the village of Çeqelê. The community has always subsisted on traditional farming, a necessity due to the mountainous terrain that makes modern tractors unusable. Historically, farmers like the renowned Arifê Hemê Heşûnê and Sereşî Hemê used ox-drawn plows, with three such plows serving the entire village, including farmers from neighboring villages like Misteko, Hec Bilêl, and Şikato. Today, they use donkeys and mules. The village's primary water source is the permanent "Kaniya Kela" (Spring of Kela), which never runs dry and once had a flour mill ("aş") operating beside it. Before the community installed a modern motor and pipe system, villagers supplemented rainwater collected in cisterns ("sahrîc") by fetching water from this spring with donkeys. The name of the larger district, Shiyeh, is derived from the Kurdish word for washing ("şûştin"), as the area's main valley was historically known as "Geliye Şiyê" (The Valley of Washing), a place where people from the region gathered weekly to wash themselves and their clothes.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Kela

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Kela

Gundê Kela is an ancient Kurdish village, described in poetry as brave, graceful, and pleasant, with a history stretching back over 400 years, a period the elders measure as "older than seven grandfathers." Its name originates from a historical castle (Keleh) upon whose ruins the village was built. This castle is tied to the local legend of Mîrê Kor (the Blind Prince), a ruler from Kela who controlled 366 villages before being defeated by the Ottoman Sultan through the deception of a beautiful woman who blinded him. The village was founded by several families, including the Dîkê, Şêx Simayîlê, and Qureyşê, who migrated from neighboring villages like Qesîmê, Qorzêlê, and Şorbe. Traditionally, the villagers lived a self-sufficient life, grinding their own wheat and bulgur with stone mills (destar), drawing water from wells (bîr) with buckets and ropes, and baking traditional breads like nanê sêlê and nanê tîr in tandoor ovens fueled by dried sheep dung. The women were skilled in handicrafts, creating hats (kofî) and decorative lace (tentele) with needles and crochet hooks, and processing raw wool (pind) with spindles (teşî) and spinning wheels (cark) to weave on looms (destgeh) for making traditional clothing. The village is also known for its agricultural bounty, including olives, grapes, pomegranates, and plums. Despite its rich history and once having 125 households, Kela has experienced significant emigration, leaving many homes empty, a sorrow often expressed through the traditional art of Dengbêjî (bardic singing), which laments the loss of homeland and the longing for traditional life.

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Kela 2

The village of Kêlê, located in the Şera district of Afrin, was originally named after the Şêxan tribe, whose ancestors founded the community. An elder from the village, 80-year-old Zuhêr Şêxo, traces his lineage back through his father, Şêx Mihemed, to his grandfather, Şêx Ebdo, and recalls a history that predates the French mandate, a time when Aghas were prominent. Traditional life in the village was characterized by self-sufficiency, with practices such as maintaining earthen roofs with a stone roller (loqê xaniyê), grinding grains like wheat and lentils with a hand millstone (destar), and plowing fields with an ox-drawn plow (hêvde). The local economy has long been tied to agriculture, particularly the cultivation and processing of figs; the yellowish fig (hêjîrê zerik) is harvested for fresh consumption and drying, while the black fig (hêjîrê reş) is used to make a traditional syrup called "şîrik." The process of drying figs involves laying them in the sun, collecting them in crates, and then stringing them. Similarly, tomato paste (dimsê bacanan) is prepared by sun-drying for winter provisions, an activity viewed as a service to the community. This traditional way of life was significantly disrupted by conflict, which led to the displacement of the villagers to Shahba, though many, like Xalê Zuhêr, have since returned to their ancestral homes, even as younger generations have begun migrating to Europe.
