I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Cela (English)

According to the book جبل الكرد|Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) Geographic Study:

Cela (Cela), جيلانلي (جيلانلي), الغزلان (الغزلان) /933n – 217h - 12km - 790m/:

Jalali (جفلالي): A Kurdish tribe found on the slopes of Ararat Mountains (جبال آرارات) and near Amadiya City (مدينة العمادية), and in the Shahrzour Region (منطقة شهرزور) in southern Kurdistan (كردستان). The Arabizer believed the name means "gazelle" from Turkish so he translated it to "Al-Ghuzlan" (الغزلان).

It is a small village located on the southern slope of "Bilaliko Mountain" (جبل بلاليكو) covered with oak forests.

According to the book عفرين.... نهرها ورواييها الخضراء|Afrin.... Her River and Green Hills:

Jilanli: A village in Kurdistan Mountain (جبل الكرد) belonging to Rajo District (ناحية راجو), Afrin Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the northern part of the mentioned mountain on the slope of the Kilsi Heights (مرتفع كلسي), and overlooks agricultural lands with alluvial soil in its southeast. It is 15 km northeast of Rajo Town (بلدة راجو). It is bounded to the north by a high mountain chain called Qara Bil Mountain (جبل /قره بيل/) and Kawenda Village (قرية /كاوندة/) and Sheikh Mohammed Li Village (/شيخ محمدلي/), to the south by a slope and agricultural plain planted with olive, vine and walnut trees and the /Bulbul – Rajo/ road and Alamdar Village (قرية / علمدار /), to the east by a valley and mountain chain and Suqanli Village (قرية /صوقانلي/), and to the west by a rugged mountain chain and valley and Jaqmaq Kabir Village (قرية /جقمق كبير/). The number of its houses is about 50 houses and its age is about 300 years (300 عاماً), knowing that the old village is located on the southern plain slope of the village, then it moved to its current high location to protect its dwellings from rain and floods. Its dwellings are stone-clay with wooden roofs and it has modern dwellings that extended towards the southwest. It has an electricity network and an elementary school. The village drinks from cisterns dug in front of the houses that collect rainwater, and currently they have started digging artesian wells for drinking. It connects with the district center by dirt road. The residents work in rainfed agriculture (olives, vines, grains, legumes, and fruit trees) on an area of 217 hectares, and raise sheep and goats. Some of them work in charcoal production from oak wood. Among the families in the village: (Al Bilal Family (آل بلال) (Bilah (بله)) - Al Mustafa Family (آل مصطفى) (Mistah (مسته)) - Al Dawoud Family (آل داود) (Dadk (دادك)) - Al Ziba Family (آل زيبه) - Al Kil Dado Family (آل كيل دادو) (Kilah (كيله))).

The village is known for its spirit of humor and joy. It is mentioned that the artist Ali Dawoud (علي داود) is from the residents of this village.

Village Headman: Shukri Ahmed Hassan (شكري أحمد حسن)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Cela

The village of Cela, founded by two brothers named Cel and Edem, traces its origins to the Celalî (Jalali) tribe who migrated from the Cizîra Botan region. According to local history, the tribe first settled near a place called Xulîlka before establishing the village, which was named after the founder, Cel. The community's foundation rests on five original families who gathered at a location known as Mist. This close-knit community life is reflected in their traditional Eid celebrations, where every family would slaughter an animal and bring the meat to the home of the mukhtar or village elder for a communal feast. This spirit of community is also seen in their seasonal food preservation, such as the making of cherry jam (Reçelê Keraza), a process involving pitting local cherries, macerating them with sugar, cooking them over an open fire, and adding lemon salt. The village's history is also marked by periods of cultural suppression; a local teacher, Ehmed Dawûd, recalls that from the 1950s through the 1980s, there were no formal schools in the village, and the Kurdish language was officially forbidden in education. The area holds ancient history, with a site known as "Taqê Bê" believed to be from the Roman era, which was used as a seasonal shelter and contains a well so deep that local folklore claims its bottom has never been reached. This rich cultural environment fostered a strong artistic heritage, with local Dengbêj (bard) Ali Dawûd beginning his journey in 1976 by learning directly from masters like Cemîl Horo, preserving the region's traditional songs through memorization and performance.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Cela 2

The village of Cela, located in the Rajo district, traces its origins to two brothers, Celal and Adem, who belonged to the Celalî tribe and migrated from Cizîra Botan. After initially settling on the hill of Xulîlka, the brothers made an agreement to divide the land, with Adem founding the village of Ademlî and Celal establishing the settlement that would be named Cela in his honor. The area holds ancient folklore, centered on a cave known as Taxê Bê (The Arch of Wind), which was said to be so warm that it emitted steam and prevented snow from settling around it in winter; local ancestors believed the cave was bottomless. Village life was marked by seasonal traditions, such as the communal making of "leçar" (jam) from local fruits like cherries, figs, and grapes, a process involving pitting the fruit, mixing it with sugar, boiling it over a fire, and adding lemon juice before jarring. In the mid-20th century, the community faced significant cultural suppression. Mamoste Çîlo (Ehmed Dawûd), a local teacher from 1951 to 1984, recalled a time when the Kurdish language was forbidden ("memnû' bû") and state officials, such as Qaid Fesîl, threatened the village mukhtar, Reşîd Hemo, accusing the Kurds of being "enemies of the state." Despite this, the villagers asserted their identity and demanded linguistic rights. This period also saw the flourishing of a rich musical heritage, with artists like Elî Dawûd beginning their careers in the 1970s and forming groups like Koma Şehîd Sefqan, which used instruments such as the tembûr, oud, and kemançe. The region's sound was heavily influenced by prominent national artists like Mamoste Cemîl Horo and Elî Tico.
