Summaries

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Qermîtliq (English)

According to the book "Mountain of the Kurds" (جبل الكرد) (Afrin City) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (الدكتور محمد عبدو علي):

Qermîtliq, Qara Matlaq, al-Khazfiya (الخزفية) /2718n, 3km - 230m/:

  • "Qarmit" (قرميت) means pottery in Kurdish. The Arabic name is a translation of its Kurdish name.
  • A large village located west of Sheikh al-Hadid town adjacent to the Turkish borders, surrounded by barbed wire from the west. The first troupe for Kurdish folk arts was established there in the sixties of the twentieth century. Beside it was a camp for Ibrahim Pasha (إبراهيم باشا]) son of [[Mohammed Ali Pasha (محمد علي باشا) during his campaigns on Syria in the nineteenth century.

According to the book: "Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills" (عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء) by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatma village (قرية قطمه):

Qarmatlaq (قرمتلق): A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد), belonging to Sheikh al-Hadid sub-district (ناحية شيخ الحديد), Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a large village located at the end of the western slope of the middle part of the mentioned mountain, in a fertile plain with volcanic soil and its lands gently slope west until they adjoin the edges of Alexandretta district (لواء اسكندرونة), 7km north-west of Sheikh al-Hadid. It is bordered to the north by fertile plain planted with olive trees and Sheikh Jiqallalli village (قرية شيخ جقاللي), to the south by Alexandretta district plain and Turkish borders, to the east by fertile plain planted with olive trees and Sheikh al-Hadid town, and to the west directly by the border strip and al-Amq plain (سهل العمق).

The number of its houses reaches about 150 houses and its age is 400 years. Its old dwellings are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs and the modern ones are concrete except the southern ones adjacent to Turkish lands. The region's settlement is very ancient as evidenced by the presence of remaining decorated pottery and tanks built of basalt and limestone stones. Its roofs are arch-shaped and the columns are basalt with crowns.

Electricity network, telephone, elementary and preparatory school and mosque in its center are available. The inhabitants work in olive, grain and legume cultivation rainfed and irrigated from artesian wells including vegetables, pomegranates, apricots, almonds, lemons in addition to raising livestock. The residents drink water from tanks and reservoirs filled with rainwater. It is an old village since the Ottoman era, and the border strip passes 500m west. It connects with the sub-district by paved road.

Among the families in Qarmatlaq village (قرية قرمتلق) are Haj Ibrahim (حاج ابراهيم) (Qartal, one of the founders of Qarmatlaq folk heritage troupe) – Baklru (بكلرو) – Darwish (درويش) (Mahmoud Darwish (محمود درويش), one of the founders of Qarmatlaq folk heritage troupe) – Dawud (داوود) (Kurdish Hanif), in addition to many holders of university degrees and institute certificates.

Village Mayor: Zakariya Haji Ibrahim (زكريا حاجي ابراهيم)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Qermîtliq

The region of Afrin, as described by Mihemed Welîd, is a historical center for the preservation of Kurdish oral traditions and Dengbêjî, a legacy attributed to its relative historical peace. This rich heritage is exemplified by folk songs such as "Eyşe Îbê," "Zeynebê," and the well-known tale of "Ferhad û Şîrîn," which the speaker personally adapted from a local story into a formal song and poem. The village of Qermîliq serves as a key historical site, being the setting for the events behind two significant Dengbêjî songs: "Xema Zalim," which recounts a fatal land dispute between two cousins of the Kosa tribe, and "Ûsivê Şer," which tells of a battle between the local Şikakî tribe and the Ottoman state. The speaker, a member of the Şikakî tribe, notes that his tribe historically occupied 44 villages in the Shera and Sherawa districts, alongside other tribes like the Jûma Shîhî and Biyan. This cultural identity is also expressed through communal traditions, such as the famous "Meryemê" song, which is accompanied by a specific dance (dîlan) style known as "Qertal" led by a "sergovend." This deep-rooted heritage continues to inform the identity of the people of Afrin, who, after being displaced to the Shahba region, find a sense of their "colorful Afrin" in the culturally similar Jazira region and actively preserve their memories through artistic expressions like an annual exhibition in Qamishlo and a 550-meter-long painting created by over 10,000 displaced children.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Qermîtliq 2

The village of Qermitlîq, located in the Shiye district of Afrin, holds a history stretching back approximately 500 years. It was originally established as a rest stop, or khan, for commercial caravans traveling the Silk Road between Aleppo and Istanbul. Its name is derived from the Kurdish word for bricks/tiles ("qermîd"), as it was a known production center for these materials, which were traded by the caravans. The village was founded by four or five families who migrated from Zorava for sheep grazing, settling near the ruins of the old khan and its 100-150 ancient wells. A significant turning point in its history occurred in 1938 with the establishment of the Syria-Turkey border, which cut the village off from all of its agricultural lands, placing them in Northern Kurdistan. While villagers could cross with permits for a time, this was completely forbidden after 1967-68 when the border was physically fortified. The community's cultural life is marked by several key institutions and traditions. The village mosque was initiated in 1344 Hijri (c. 1925) by Şêx Hemo and completed by the community after his death. In 1958, Qermitliq became home to the first folklore troupe in the Afrin region, "Koma Çiyayê Kurmênc," which performed widely until a planned trip to Egypt was cancelled due to the dissolution of the United Arab Republic. The village also maintains ancient traditions, such as visiting the 500-year-old "Dara Merazê" (Tree of Wishes) on Wednesdays to make vows, a practice believed to date back to Zoroastrian times. Additionally, the villagers prepare a special ancestral dish called "Ketoye," made from pounded wheat, for both weddings and funerals.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Qermîtliq

The village of Qermtalqê, also referred to as Qermilqê, is a community of approximately 300 houses located in a hilly, olive-growing region of Afrin near the Turkish border. It is part of a network of interconnected villages that includes Kerke Jorîn, Kerke Jêr, Şiyê, Gundê Xelîl, Dêrmîş, Ondecî, Badîna, and Kêlakê, whose residents consider each other neighbors, highlighting the strong social ties in the area. The cultural life of the village is marked by deep-rooted traditions of hospitality, where guests are welcomed with blessings of prosperity and the phrase "on our heads, on our eyes." An elder from the community offered a traditional blessing for the village's protection from "calamities and troubles." The architecture includes notable modern stone houses, such as the ornate home of the Hesen Hesen family, which features a large, landscaped courtyard with decorative tile inlays. The widespread use of solar panels on rooftops indicates the integration of modern technology into the village's infrastructure.
