I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Dumilya (English)

According to the book جبل الكرد|Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (الدكتور محمد عبدو علي):

Dumilya (Dumilya), دومبللي (دومبللي), الأمسية (الأمسية) /2148n - 232h - 12km - 600m/:

Damliya (دملياa): A corruption of the name Donbllali (دونبلالي), which is the name of a Kurdish tribe found in Botan (بوتان) /Lêrx (ليرخ), p.48/, which lived under Safavid rule (الحكم الصفوي), then migrated to the Ottoman section (القسم العثماني) of Kurdistan (كردستان), and settled west of Diyarbakir (ديار بكر) and established in the city Palo (Palo) and its surroundings a semi-independent emirate /Kurdish Dictionary (قاموس كوراني)/. Danbal (دنبل) in Kurdish means hedgehog. According to 'كتاب المسالك' 'Book of Routes', the Danbali people (الدنبلية) were a people who inhabited the Inverted Mountains (جبال المقلوب) and Al-Mukhtar (المختار), and the persecution drove them to leave for the areas of Mosul (الموصل) and Azerbaijan (أذربيجان), and they were able, thanks to their cunning, to establish an independent administration in Kurdistan (كردستان) and Azerbaijan (أذربيجان). The Sharafnama (شرفنامة) says about them that they are from the Ezidi sect (النحلة الإيزيدية). Amin Zaki (أمين زكي) J1 p.403/, says this tribe is from Parazi (البرازية). Wasfi Zakaria (وصفي زكريا) says about them that they are from the Milli tribe (القبيلة المللية) /p.664/. The Arabized name is given by the Arabization Committee (لجنة التعريب).

It is a medium-sized village located at the foothills of the southeastern slope of Benê Mountain (جبل Benê), and is 2 km west of Badanli Village (ق.بعدنلي).

According to the book Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatma Village (قرية قطمه):

Dombllali: A village in Kurdistan Mountain (جبل الكرد) belonging to Rajo District (ناحية راجو), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a large village located on the southern slope (mountainous elevation and slope) of the central part of the mentioned mountain. It is bounded to the north by a high and rugged mountain chain and Darwish Oba Si Village (قرية درويش أوبه سي), to the south by a slope and valley and Kurkan Tahtani Village (قرية /كوركان تحتاني/) and Kurkan Foqani Village (/كوركان فوقاني/), to the west by a high and rugged mountain chain and Quda Koy Village (قرية /قوده كوي/), and to the east 500 m away Badanli Village (قرية /بعدنلي/). The number of its houses is about 200 houses and its age is about 400 years (400 عاماً). Its old dwellings are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs and the modern concrete ones have spread west, south and east. It has an electricity network and water network from an artesian well belonging to the state, and the village used to drink from a Roman well in the past. It has an elementary school and a mosque. The village connects with the district and region by paved roads. The residents work in rainfed agriculture (olives, vines, grains, and other fruit trees) in addition to raising sheep and goats. The village is considered one of the beautiful villages in terms of construction and location.

Village Headman: Bakr Ali Bakr (بكر علي بكر)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Dumilya

Gundê Dimilya, located in the Mabeta district of Afrin, was founded approximately 500 years ago by three Zaza individuals who migrated from the Mazîya mountains in the Mardin province of Northern Kurdistan. The founders were two brothers, Ma'm and Us, and their maternal uncle, Mele, who traveled via the Ahras/Ehrez route and established the village, naming it after their Dimilî tribe. Originally shepherds who utilized local caves, their community grew to around 360 households. The village's historical language was Zazaki, a dialect the current inhabitants have since forgotten, though they maintain a strong cultural identity known for patriotism and sacrifice, with many villagers martyred in the "freedom revolution." A significant historical artifact is the village's ancient olive press stone, or Delîb, which is estimated to be 160-170 years old and was brought from Tell Jarnûs. For generations, this stone was powered by donkeys to crush local olives for oil and wheat for bulgur. In modern times, this tradition has evolved with the establishment of a local olive processing factory, founded by a villager who returned from Aleppo. This factory now processes various types of local olives, which are sold throughout the Jazira region and exported as far as America.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Dumilya 2

The village of Dumilya (Dimilya), located in the Mabeta district of Afrin, was founded approximately 500 years ago by three Zaza-speaking relatives from the Dimilya tribe who migrated from the Mazîya Mountain region in Northern Kurdistan. The founders—brothers Mem and Os, along with their maternal cousin Mela—were shepherds who crossed the Ahrez River and settled at the site after discovering a cave with a water source. At the time of their arrival, the area was also inhabited by the Sheikhan tribe. Lacking a formal shrine (ziyaret), the villagers developed a unique spiritual tradition centered on a sacred rock called "Kevirê Hêvî" (The Stone of Hope), where they perform rituals to pray for rain during droughts; this involves sacrificing a chicken, anointing the stone with its blood, and preparing a communal meal of bulgur. The village's long history of agriculture is also represented by an ancient stone press (delîb), estimated to be 160-170 years old, which was brought from Til Cernos and was used for processing olives, bulgur, and wheat.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Dumilya

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Dumilya

In the Afrin region, the annual celebration of Newroz on March 21st is regarded as a sacred and foundational "Eid" (holiday) with deep, ancient roots, serving as a vital symbol for the Kurmanj people. The tradition is consciously continued as a way to honor ancestral practices and ensure the preservation of Kurdish existence, language, and memory. Preparations are a significant community effort, particularly in villages like Dêmilo and Ba'dînê, where for a period of four to ten days, the entire community, including children and youth, works hard to gather materials for the celebratory bonfire. This practice has evolved over time; elders recall gathering natural materials like wood and thorns in their childhood, while the modern tradition involves the collection of approximately 350 tires, which are hauled up the mountainside from the villages below. This communal labor is complemented by cultural expressions, such as the singing of traditional children's songs, which are passed down orally through the generations.

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Dumilya 2

Life in the village of Dûmilya was traditionally characterized by hard work, self-sufficiency, and a strong sense of community. The villagers were deeply connected to their land, engaging in farming (cotkarî) that included the cultivation of olives (zeytûn) and vineyards (rezan), and practicing skills like grafting (vedan). This self-reliance extended to all aspects of life; they produced their own food, including traditional wheat bread (nanê genimî) baked on a convex griddle known as a sêl with specific tools like the qeyax, tîr, and pande, as well as other foods like bûşî, qirnawî, and çeqelme. Clothes (kinc) were also made by hand through spinning (teşî dirêst), a necessary skill when families lacked money or gold to purchase them. Social life was vibrant, with community members regularly gathering in front of their homes on stone benches (ber sûfê) and in courtyards (ber paxara) to socialize, a practice that has since faded. Marriage customs allowed for couples from the village to meet and fall in love before marrying, and wedding celebrations would continue right up to the wedding day itself, without the bride and groom seeing each other during this final period. The elders recall this past life as being healthier, attributing a decline in well-being to the introduction of modern conveniences like store-bought bakery bread.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Dumilya

The village of Demîlo is a place of deep-rooted cultural traditions and poignant family histories, as documented through a significant reunion and daily life. The central event is the emotional return of a woman named Latîfa to her mother and sisters after a 30-year separation, a period during which she lived in Aleppo and Qamishli. This long absence is lamented by the sisters as a consequence of a broader tragedy that has befallen their people ("xelk") and homeland ("welat"), a separation they attribute to "fate" ("qeder"). The village's social fabric is highlighted by the gathering of its elders ("Cemaetê kalên Demîlo"), who sit together and exchange blessings, including a prayer for the salvation of the Kurdish nation. Within the family home, the tradition of remembrance is evident through the display of framed photographs of ancestors from past generations. The visit also captures the communal preparation of traditional "Komb" cookies, where multiple women work together on the floor, shaping dough with molds, arranging them on metal trays with handwritten paper liners, sprinkling them with sesame seeds, and baking them in a small oven, preserving a vital aspect of their cultural heritage.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Dumilya 2

The village of Dumilya is located in a rural, hilly region of Afrin, characterized by extensive olive groves. The area holds significant memories for its youth, particularly along a path referred to as the "lower line." The village's cemetery is situated nearby, within the area of Pata village. The broader landscape includes the region of Rêşa Karko, which contains the Karko school as well as the villages of Upper and Lower Karko. This specific location serves a modern communal purpose, as it is a place where local people can access the Turkish mobile network to contact relatives living in Europe. A key feature of the local infrastructure is a "sarinc," or cistern, which was built by the community and is used as a water source for both people and their animals. The video also identifies a cluster of other nearby villages, including Maydankê, Ba'dîna, Avîna, Qeta, Kêlîbo, Sêwiya, and Hebo, which has its own reservoir.
