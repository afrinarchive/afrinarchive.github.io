I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Mabeta (English)

Mabetli District (ناحية معبطلي) consists of 42 administrative divisions. Its center is Mabetli town (بلدة معبطلي). Its boundaries: from the north Rajo District (ناحية راجو), from the west Sheikh al-Hadid District (ناحية شيخ الحديد), from the south Jindires District (ناحية جندريس), and from the east Afrin City Center (المركز- عفرين) and Sharan Districts (شران).

  • The town is located on a calcareous plateau that slopes steeply toward the north, west, and east, connecting in the west with Qantarah village (قرية قنطرة). Beautiful buildings in the form of beautiful villas have begun to appear in it, and currently there is not much space left on the plateau for the town's expansion.

It has simple commercial establishments and a workshop for maintaining machinery and European blacksmithing. The inhabitants work in agriculture, especially olives and grapevines. It has a recruitment branch and a health center in addition to the municipality and district administration. Mabetli (معبطلي) was the center of "Kurd Dagh" (كرد داغ) district at the beginning of the French mandate over Syria.

According to the book: 'عفرين.... نهرها ورواbiها الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills' by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatmeh village (قرية قطمه):

Al-Mabetli (المعبطلي): A town in Kurd Mountain (جبل الكرد) and is a district center, belonging to Afrin City Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a large town located in the central section of the mentioned mountain, on a wavy and elongated calcareous plateau from east to west with an elevation of /560m/, covered with olive and natural pine trees. The plateau slopes steeply toward the north, west, and east, connecting in the west with Qantarah village (قرية قنطرة) and from the north with Qitranli village (قرية قطرانلي). Its soil is clay-based and water streams cross it from several directions. It is /15km/ northwest of Afrin City (مدينة عفرين). To the north it is bordered by a slope and water stream and mountain elevation and Qitranli village (قرية قطرانلي) nearby at 1km distance, to the south by a slope and wide valley and mountain elevations and several streams and Mirkanli village (قرية ميركانلي), to the east by a steep slope and several streams planted with olive trees and Kokan Foqani and Tahtani villages (قرية كوكان فوقاني وتحتاني), and to the west at a distance of several meters Qantarah village (قرية قنطرة) connected to the town and at /2km/ distance Arab Oshaghi village (قرية عرب أوشاغي). It contains approximately 750 houses (بيتاً) and is about 500 years old (عاماً). According to the civil registry records of the town at the end of 2004, the population was about 7235 souls, and the total population of Mabetli District (ناحية معبطلي) is /52536/ souls.

Its old houses are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, and modern construction has expanded, especially toward the southeast, and beautiful buildings have begun to appear in the form of villas consisting of several floors spread beautifully on the plateau, and currently there is not much space left due to the expansion of construction in the town.

There is a valley in the middle of the town dividing it into two sections (northern and southern). It has an electricity network and a water network connected to a tank from a well at the bottom of the town from the western side, which belongs to the state. There are also several official departments in the town that are available in the district center (police station, telephone center, municipality building, party branch, consumer institution, agricultural association, and several modern olive presses, and a group of primary, preparatory, and secondary schools and a center for the district directorate and recruitment branch and a mosque in the center of the village).

The inhabitants work in cultivating rain-fed grains (legumes, grapevines, olives, and other fruit trees). There is also irrigated cultivation on an area of 25ha irrigated with artesian wells of summer vegetables, walnuts, and pomegranates alongside raising sheep, goats, and cattle. Some people also work in light food industries such as olive pressing and dairy products, drying vegetables and fruits, and others outside the town in various institutions and jobs in major cities. There is also a commercial center for marketing the town and its surroundings. It is connected to the region center by a paved road, and passes through its center to several villages. Among its most important families is the family of Ali Sheikh Ismail Zadeh Agha (علي شيخ إسماعيل زاده آغا) and others who were the first to inhabit the village since ancient times.

Mabetli Town (بلدة المعبطلي): A town located in Kurd Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Afrin City Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب). It is a district center, bordered to the north by Bulbul (بلبل) and Rajo Districts (راجو), to the west by Sheikh Hadid District (ناحية شيخ حديد), to the south by Jindires District (ناحية جندريس) and Sheikh al-Hadid villages (قرى شيخ الحديد), and to the east by Sharan District (ناحية شران) and Afrin City Center District (ناحية مركز عفرين). The town consists of several villages and farms and includes (34) villages and (10 farms), all its lands are clay-based and mountainous planted with forest trees, olives, grapevines, walnuts, and almonds.

Among the holders of higher education in the village: Hassan Qalandar (حسن قلندر) / Doctorate in Statistics and Computing / France - Abdul Hamid Qalandar (عبدالحميد قلندر) / Doctorate in Food Industries / France - Azad Hamotu (آزاد حموتو) / Doctorate in Archaeology and History / Western Germany

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Mabeta

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ryF5tfYVOc0

The town of Mabeta, located 17 kilometers from the city of Efrîn in the region of Çiyayê Kurmênc, is a prominent center for Elewî Kurds. The name Mabeta is locally interpreted to stem from the word malbat (families), referencing the town's origins as a settlement formed by the unification of several distinct lineages. According to local elders, the first family to settle the area was the Qarpûz family, followed by the Alo, Açûlo, Gûzeleko, Mehmûd Aliyo, and Seyîd Xaço families. Today, the town consists of approximately 700 to 800 households and sustains itself primarily through Zeytûn (olive) cultivation, despite suffering from a scarcity of water sources in the immediate vicinity.

The spiritual focal point of Mabeta is the shrine of Dede Elî, a holy figure revered as a welî. The site is historically significant and is associated with the local Elewî identity, which residents assert was once the dominant faith of the entire Efrîn and Maraş region before Osmanî (Ottoman) policies forced many toward the Sunni sect. The shrine of Dede Elî is also known by the title Pîrê Baranê (The Elder of the Rain). This title originated from a miraculous event during a drought when villagers gathered at the shrine to pray; rain reportedly fell immediately but stopped exactly at the boundary of the shrine's trees. The site is also a destination for healing. One account describes a man from Xims who dreamt of the shrine and traveled there to perform a sacrifice, which resulted in the curing of his son's Pênceşêr (cancer).

The inhabitants of Mabeta view their history as deeply ancient, citing the presence of local ruins and stones as evidence of their connection to the Hûrî, Mîtanî, and Bîzantîn civilizations, whom they regard as the ancestors of the Kurds. Culturally, the town preserves specific local legends, such as the story of Tûsin o (real name Ebdik), an outlaw figure remembered in song. This legend is tied to the visit of a historical figure named Ehmed Agha Şêx Ismaîl.

Religious life in Mabeta is guided by leaders such as Şêx Cûmerd, who identifies the community's faith with the Bektaşî path and the love of the Ehlûl Beyt. He emphasizes the spiritual light of Îmam Elî and the lineage of figures like Fatime, Hesen, and Huseyn. While Mabeta is a central hub, residents note that other nearby villages such as Alkana, Gundê Xelîl, and Xidiriya also contain shrines and tombs of Dedes, preserving the region's religious heritage.

Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Mabeta

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jV5LUWyIBYs

The substantial town of Mabeta, located in the Afrin region, is depicted as a historical and administrative hub with a lineage stretching back over seven centuries. According to the host, the settlement is more than 700 years old. There is a specific discourse regarding the etymology of the name Mabeta. The host suggests it evolved from "Malbeta," referring to the four original families who settled there. However, a local elder and primary interviewee, Ismet (also addressed as Ebû Hesen from the Reşkole family), corrects this interpretation. He states the name is derived from "Mehebet," meaning love or affection. He explains that historically, if people were in conflict or could not sleep due to grievances, they would come to this location to reconcile, thus earning it the name associated with peace and affection.

The social structure of Mabeta is defined by four foundational families. Ismet lists these primary lineages as the Alo, the Qarpûza, the Gûzeleka, and the Kopiziya families. These groups are considered the most famous and historical inhabitants. Over time, the population grew significantly, reaching approximately 8,000 people in the past, though migration and current circumstances have reduced this number to roughly 5,000 residents. Other specific family names mentioned during the tour include Seydî Tûpê (specifically a member named Habû), Ehmelî, Mistî 'Eşînû, Ebû Omer, Ebû Ciwan, and the Bilêl family (specifically Mihemedî Bilêl, Hecî Bilêl, and 'Arfî Bilêl).

Mabeta is distinguished by its size and development, described more as a town or small city than a village. It serves as a district center (nahiye) with multi-story buildings reaching four to six floors, paved roads, and educational facilities extending to the baccalaureate level. Economically, the area is known for its olives (zeytûna), vineyards (reza), and fruit trees. The documentary features a visit to the home of Ebû Hesen, showcasing a lush courtyard filled with vegetables and fruit trees, which he refers to locally as "kender."

In a discussion on identity and origins, Ebû Hesen asserts a deep historical lineage for the people of the region. He rejects modern political labels, stating, "We are Hûrî, we are Mîtanî," referring to the ancient Hurrian and Mitanni civilizations. He claims that the exonym "Kurd" was applied to them by Greek philosophers or Romans to signify "lions" or "lions of the mountains," but emphasizes their indigenous roots as Hûrî. The narrative concludes with a call for the diaspora in Europe to return and invest in local projects, such as battery factories or agricultural infrastructure, to support the remaining population.
