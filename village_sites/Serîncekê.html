Se’rîncek, سعرنجك (Sa'ranjak) – سعركانلي (Sa'arkanli), Al-Sa'i [The Runner] /752 - 840 AD - 15 km - 600 m/

  • Se’rîncek: means "the cold water spring" with the addition of the Kurdish name-forming suffix "ek" to the name. The Arabization of the name is arbitrary, as the meaning "Al-Sa'i" in Arabic also has no connection.

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Serîncekê (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City / Geographic Study:

Se'rîncek, Sa'ranjak (سعرنجك) – Sa'rkanli (سعركانلي), Al-Sa'r (السعر) /752n - 840h - 15km - 600m/:

  • Se'rîncek: Means "underground water tank" with the addition of the Kurdish diminutive suffix ek to the name. The Arabization of the name is arbitrary, as there is no meaning for "sa'r" in Arabic either.
  • A small village located on the western bank of the Afrin River (عفرين) about 3km away, facing Mount Sheikhurz (شيخورز) to the north.

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء' 'Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills':

Sa'ranjak (سعرنجك): A village in Jabal al-Akrad (جبل الكرد) belonging to Sheran District (شران), Afrin (عفرين) region, Aleppo Governorate (حلب), (734 people).

It is a small village located on an elevation and limestone plateau that slopes toward the south and a mountainous slope. It is connected by a plain, 27km from the town of Sheran (شران), and is located on the Sheikh Khurzus Mountain (الشيخ خوروز) chain. It is bordered to the north by a high and rugged mountainous chain planted with oak trees and the village of Sheikh Khurzus (الشيخ خوروز), to the south by a slope and plain planted with olive trees with two streams starting from the north and ending in the south at the Afrin River (عفرين) valley and the village of Ballursank (بللورسنك), to the west by a valley and high mountainous chain and the villages of Qazeh Bash (قزه باش) and Bilan Kuyi (بيلان كوي), and to the east by a valley and the village of Omar Simo (عمر سيمو). It has about 65 houses and is approximately 300 years old. Its old houses are stone and clay with wooden roofs, and the modern ones are stone and cement. It is one of the old villages in Sheran District (شران). It has an electricity network and drinking water from an artesian well. It has an elementary school and a paved road in the village center that connects to Prophet Houri (النبي هوري) and the road connecting to the village is a mountainous dirt road. There are also three modern villas on the western side of the village and a modern technical olive press. Its residents work in olive and grape cultivation alongside raising sheep and goats.

It is noted that the artist Jamil Horo (جميل هورو) is from the sons of this village.

Village mayor: Horik Hanan (هوريك حنان)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Serîncekê

Located in the Shera (Şera) district, the village of Serincekê traces its origins back approximately 400 years when nomadic families, owners of sheep and cattle, settled in the plains at a site whose ruins are now called Sêmal ka. The village's name itself means "place of small springs," derived from the sêrincek (small wells) that the founding families—including Malê Lîko, Malê Reşê Ereb, Malê Omer, and Malê Qemer Axa—dug to solve the area's water scarcity. Over time, the community grew as other families arrived from Turkey and the nearby village of Belîlko, all living together harmoniously. Historically, the Malê Lîko family served as the village elders (rûspî) and maintained a communal guest house (oda), a center for social life and dispute resolution. The village is also remembered as the home of the renowned singer Cemîl Horo and for its significant resistance against the French Mandate, led by figures such as Şêx Henîf and Qurt Elî. This resistance, which organized a force stretching to Meydan, was met with French aerial bombardment that killed local heroes, including Mistefa Çolax and Ehmedê Rûtê. A tangible link to this past is a large stone mortar (sokî), estimated to be 300-400 years old, which was brought from the Şikefta Bîra Tûyê cave and used communally for pounding grain to the rhythmic chant of "deh, dûh." This tool, along with a now-ruined mill (dêlib), was central to village life before modern machinery. Traditional wedding customs were also rich, involving a ceremony called gawirîn where families would formally agree to the marriage, followed by the exchange of gifts known as xelat and berdanî. A notable past tradition involved the bride riding a horse to her new home with a young boy seated behind her, a symbolic wish for her to bear sons.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Serîncekê 2

The village of Serîncekê, located in the Shera district, derives its name from the Kurdish word for "small spring" ("serîncek"), a reference to the wells its nomadic, pastoralist founders dug for their livestock upon settling the area. The original settlement was situated about two kilometers away in the plains at a site now known as Xirabê Sê Mal (The Ruins of Three Houses), which contained landmarks like Bîra Şadiya (Şadiya's Well) and Bîra Tûyê (The Mulberry Well). The village was established by five principal families—Mala Lîko, Mala Reşê Ereb, Mala Omer, and Mala Qemer Oxlî—and later grew with the arrival of families from Turkey and the village of Belîlko. A significant part of the village's oral history involves the resistance against the French Mandate, led by local figures such as Şêx Henîf and Qurt Elî, which culminated in an aerial bombardment of the village by French forces. A central cultural site is Keleha Şêr (Lion's Castle), whose name originates from the tradition of women bringing milk ("şîr") to the location, which also featured a court and a "Rumbling Rock" (Kevirê Gumgumê) that was a source of water. Traditional practices remain part of village life, including the communal pounding of grain in a large stone mortar ("sûq") to a rhythmic chant of "deh" and "dûh," and vibrant wedding celebrations that feature traditional music, line dancing, and wrestling.
