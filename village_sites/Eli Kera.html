I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City (عفرين) / Geographical Study:

Elîkera (Elîkera), عليكار, علي الأطرش /699N - 130ha - 5km - 990m/:

  • Meaning "lame Ali" (علي الأطرش), and the Arabized name is a translation from the folk designation.
  • A small village located above the eastern slope of Mount Girê Çûçik where springs are abundant around it.

According to the book 'عفرين.... نهرها ورواباتها الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills':

عليكار: A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد) belonging to Bilbil (بلبل) district, Afrin City (عفرين) region, Aleppo (حلب) governorate.

It is a small village located above a rocky plateau on the lower northern slope of the small "Damerik" (داميريك) mountain with limestone rocks and covered in parts with green and basalt rocks. It is 5km northwest of Bilbil (بلبل). Springs are abundant around it and a watercourse descends from it toward the northeast where there are agricultural lands with fertile volcanic soil. It is bordered to the north by mountain elevations planted with forest trees and the village of "Bik Oba Si" (بيك أوبه سي), and to the south 500m away is the village of "Heyamli" (هيامليl), and to the west by a high mountain chain planted with forest trees and the village of "Kawandeh" (كاوندة), and to the east by a slope and deep water valley and Za'reh (زعرة) village. The number of its houses is about 35 houses and its age is about 350 years. Its old houses are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs and the modern ones are cement in its north and south. There is an electricity network and elementary school available. Most of its inhabitants work in olive and grain cultivation irrigated on 130 hectares, and nuts, almonds, and pomegranates irrigated from springs, in addition to raising sheep and goats. Others work in nearby marble quarries. It drinks from spring water and from cisterns where rainwater collects. Currently the residents have equipped the spring with pipes to prevent water waste and use it only for drinking. It is connected to Bilbil (بلبل) town by a paved road and passes through its center to several neighboring villages.

Village mayor: Nouri Mohammed Ali (نوري محمد علي)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

The village of Alî Kerê, located near Bilbilê in the Efrîn region (01:52), was reportedly founded by a man named Elî, who came from east of Girê Çûçik mountain (02:04-02:07). He was hard of hearing, leading to the name "Alî Kerê" (meaning Deaf Ali) (02:11-02:13, 03:20-03:22). Elî migrated from what is now Turkey (North Kurdistan) (03:25-03:31). During the Ottoman era, the village, also known as El Aibîyan, was subject to the court of Kilis (02:21-02:27). Before the modern border was established, villagers primarily interacted with Kilis (02:30-02:39); afterward, connections shifted to Bilbilê (02:43-02:45). Following Elî's settlement, other families migrated to the village over time, including descendants of Hisênê Kêlê (from Turkey) (04:37-04:45), families from Mola Qotû (05:07-05:11), Molê Çapo (from Bîkê village) (05:33-05:37), and Molê Hebeş (from Lehîyo) (05:40-05:41). A cemetery near the village, containing the old shrine (ziyaret) of Şêx Hemz (23:09, 33:09), was historically shared with villages now across the border in Turkey, such as Kilûrê/Kilorê and Çerço (25:30-25:44). The creation of the border fence ended this shared use (06:17-06:27, 25:44-25:52). Past village life involved strong communal traditions, like collective cooking and eating during festivals under a large village tree or in prominent families' homes in winter (08:14-08:21, 10:15-10:45), a practice that faded roughly 20-25 years prior to the recording (09:30-09:35). Villagers historically practiced mutual aid, such as helping build each other's mud-and-poplar-beam houses (11:19-11:51). The main village spring was relocated closer to the village center long ago by the elders using ancient stones ("kewirêd asorî") for construction (14:34-15:25, 17:31-18:38), and was renovated again about 15 years before the recording (19:22). Before the Syrian revolution, many villagers had moved to cities; the conflict caused many to return (37:40-37:53). An anecdote recalls a fatal accident involving celebratory gunfire during a double wedding held long ago (12:36-13:25).
