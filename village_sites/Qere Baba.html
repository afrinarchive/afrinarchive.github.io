I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Qere Baba (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin / Geographic Study:

Qerebaba (قره بابا), Al-Sawda (السوده) /942N - 430H - 11km - 480m/:

  • Qere Baba (قره بابا): Originally a Turkish name meaning "the black father". The Arabic name is an incomplete translation of the Turkish naming.
  • This medium-sized village is located on an elevated summit that slopes toward the Black River (النهر الأسود) course to the west. It is 1 km from the Turkish border (الحدود التركية).

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء' 'Afrin .... Its Rivers and Green Hills':

Qere Baba (قره بابا): A village in Jabal al-Kurd (جبل الكرد) belonging to Rajo District (ناحية راجو), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located on the elevation of the western slope of Jabal al-Kurd (جبل الكرد) near the Turkish border (حدود تركيا) from the northwestern direction of Afrin City region on a fertile mountainous slope with olive trees around it. To the north, a slope planted with forest and olive trees with Medan Ikbas Town (بلدة ميدان إكبس). To the south, a deep slope and plain with the Black River (النهر الأسود) course and direct Turkish border (حدود تركيا). To the west, a slope and the Black River (النهر الأسود) and Turkish border (حدود تركيا) 500m away. To the east, a fertile plain with the public road (Rajo (راجو) - Medan Ikbas (ميدان إكبس)) and Firfirik Tahtani Village (قرية فرفريك تحتاني) and Saluk Village (قرية سلوك).

It has approximately 40 houses and is about 350 years old according to the village head. Its old dwellings are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, and recently cement houses have spread to the east and south. It has an electrical network and drinking water from an artesian well and an elementary school. It connects to the main road by a paved dirt road.

Residents work in rain-fed agriculture (olives, grapes, grains) and irrigation from the Black River (النهر الأسود) (summer vegetables, cotton, watermelon, sunflowers, apples, walnuts, cherries, etc.). Among its most important families: Bakr Agha Family (عائلة بكر آغا) who are the original owners of the village. Its fertile soil is due to its proximity to the Black River (النهر الأسود).

Village Head: Mahmoud Muhammad Hanan (محمود محمد حنان)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Qere Baba

The village of Qerebaba, located in the Rajo region on the Turkish-Syrian border, has a history stretching back over 500 years. According to local elders, the settlement was founded by two primary families, Malbata Elî Gulemê and Malbata Mela Mextêd, and its name originates from a pre-existing shrine, "Ziyareta Qerebaba," which itself bears a Turkish name from the long Ottoman presence in the area. The village's life and location have been dictated by its water sources; it was originally established on an ancient spring of the "Ava Reş" (Black Water), and when the spring's flow diminished around the year 2000, the community relied on the nearby Ava Reş river, which flows from Kurmanc Mountain, for irrigating their olive groves and gardens. A significant historical event occurred in 1939 when, according to an eyewitness, French and Turkish authorities established the border, physically cutting Qerebaba off from its sister village, Çeqela, in Northern Kurdistan and resulting in the seizure of villagers' agricultural lands on the other side. This border also separated the community from historical structures like the Pira Heşarkê (Bridge of Heşarkê), built during the French Mandate. The village maintained strong communal traditions, such as the Eid celebration held at the historic house of Mela Bekir Axa, where each family would bring food to share. More recently, the Syrian government officially changed the village's name to "Al-Sawda" as part of an Arabization policy.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Qere Baba 2

The village of Qerebaba, located in the Rajo district of Afrin, is an ancient settlement estimated to be over 500 years old, its identity and name derived from the shrine (Ziyareta Qerebaba) of a revered figure remembered in oral tradition as a martyr or dervish from the Ottoman era. The village's history and daily life have been profoundly shaped by its location on the border between West (Rojava) and North (Bakur) Kurdistan, a division formalized in 1931 by French and Turkish authorities. This border cut Qerebaba off from neighboring villages like Çeqela, separating families who were once relatives and depriving them of their agricultural lands on the other side, which were seized by the state. The community's social life traditionally centered around a historic spring (kanî), which originates from a cave (şkeft) and was developed from a well by a villager named Heyderê Mixtêr, and a large, ancient terebinth tree where villagers held evening gatherings (şevbêrk) for storytelling. This tradition declined with the advent of electricity and television. The village also contains a mosque, built by the community 20-30 years ago on the site of an old stone courtyard, which houses historical books. Reflecting the political pressures of the region, the village's original Turkish name was officially changed to the Arabic "Al-Sawda" by the Syrian government approximately two decades before this documentary was filmed.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Qere Baba

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Qere Baba

The village of Qere Baba, situated in a high, elevated location, holds an oral history of being founded by four ancestors: Qere, who was said to be Turkish; Reşo, who was Kurdish; Dada, the grandfather of a current resident's mother-in-law; and a fourth unnamed founder. Key landmarks include the village crossroads, a shrine (Ziyareta Qere Baba) for the deceased, a mosque named Masjid Khatun, and an ancient spring (kaniya) that has existed since the time of the ancestors and never runs dry. This spring, which once overflowed into a nearby river during rainy seasons, was a central social hub with walnut trees and canopies. The village also contains old stone houses, with at least one being built in 1952 by a man named Tîmorê Rewanî. However, the village has faced significant hardship in modern times. Many of the old houses have been damaged by an earthquake, and the community has suffered from depopulation due to war. This decline is reflected at the spring, which is no longer a social gathering place but is now used primarily for drinking water for the few remaining residents and their animals.
