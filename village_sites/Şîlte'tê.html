I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City / Geographic Study:

Şîlte't, Sheltah (شلتاح) /280n - 300h - 15km - 430m/:

  • Sheltah (شلتاح): The origin of the word "Sheltah" (شلتاح), meaning "blue rocks" (الصخور الزرقاء), but the letter "l" was replaced with "n", which is common in the Kurdish language. The rocks of those areas are of different colors, with what appears near the Afrin River (عفرين) tending toward an ash-gray color mixed with blue, making them appear bright blue.
  • A small village located on a plateau that slopes steeply toward the Afrin River (عفرين) valley to the east, bordering the Turkish borders from the north. Around it are archaeological ruins and caves.

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء' 'Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills':

Sheltah (شلتاح): A village in Jabal al-Akrad (جبل الكرد) belonging to Sheran District (شران), Afrin (عفرين) region, Aleppo Governorate (حلب), (263 people).

It is a small village located on a wide and undulating limestone plateau that slopes steeply toward the Afrin River (عفرين) valley to the east, and gently toward the south and west. It is connected by a plain where pastures spread above it. It is 15km from the town of Sheran (شران) in the northeastern direction, and borders the current borders with Turkey. The region's construction is ancient, evidenced by the presence of ruins and caves around it and near the Turkish borders from the eastern side. It is bordered to the north by a deep valley and the point of the Turkish-Syrian border directly at 200m distance, to the south by a valley and fertile plain and the course of the Afrin River (عفرين), to the east by a slope and valley and the course of the Afrin River (عفرين) and the point of the Turkish-Syrian border, and to the west by a valley and fertile agricultural plain planted with olive trees and the village of Deir Sawan (دير صوان) and the ancient Prophet Houri Bridge (جسر النبي هوري) named after Prophet Houri (النبي هوري). It has about 35 houses and the village is approximately 150 years old. Its old houses are stone and clay with wooden roofs, and the modern residences are cement and stone. It has an electricity network and the village drinks from a water network connected to artesian wells in its eastern direction and a paved road to the village center and Marsawa Village (مرساوا). It has an elementary school, a mosque, and a border police station. Its residents work in olive and grape cultivation and livestock raising. It has telephone lines and a modern olive press located near the Roman bridge, 3 kilometers from the bridge. It is a border village on the eastern side of Afrin City.

Village mayor: Mohammed Hamo (محمد حمو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

The village of Şîltahetê is stated to be approximately 100 years old (04:06). Its name, Şîltahetê, meaning "wet hard flat ground," is derived from its geography, as the land is always moist (03:32-03:36, 10:28-10:34). The initial inhabitants were people referred to as "ûxlanê Hecî Emerlî ya" from Bakurê Kurdistanê (northern Kurdistan) (03:42-03:46). Before settling in Şîltahetê, these groups reportedly migrated from Bakurê Kurdistanê to a ruined village named Bekerê, then to Qernê, and subsequently to Kilisê, all before the modern borders were established (03:46-04:01). The first three primary families to permanently settle in Şîltahetê came from Dêrsîwanê (06:01-06:10). Later, other families arrived from various places including Qoşa, Qurta, Emera, and Berkoşê, with many belonging to the Rûşkî tribe and having a Koçer (nomadic) background (06:16-06:33, 06:49-06:55). Historically, before the current borders were drawn, Şîltahetê was administratively connected to Kilisê (10:44-10:48). The Afrin River historically enters the Rojava region first through Şîltahetê (04:36-04:43, 40:14-40:20). In terms of agriculture, approximately 40 years ago, cotton (pembo) was cultivated in Şîltahetê (08:51-08:54, 32:19-32:23), and tobacco was also grown in the past (09:00-09:13). A fishpond and restaurant were established in the area in the 1980s (11:43). The tradition of making kunefe (a sweet pastry) in the village was influenced by Kilisê, with people or influences from a place called Eşêftox near Kilisê bringing the practice (23:01-23:13). The transcript also details old wedding customs, particularly the "hinacîye" (henna night), including how brides were escorted (often by horse before cars), songs were sung, and gifts were exchanged (33:24-37:16).
