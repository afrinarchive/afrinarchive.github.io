Fafirtîn, Fafirtin, 735/ - 35 km - 550m:

  • 'Abdallah Hajjar believes that its name is Syriac, meaning the village of "fig fruit."

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Fafirtîn (English)

According to the book جبل الكرد|Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) geographical study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Fafirtîn, فافرتين /735N - 35km - 550m/:

  • Abdullah Hajjar believes it is a Syriac name meaning "village of fig fruits."
  • A small village among the villages of Shirwan. It contains remains of the oldest church altar documented in Syria from the fourth century AD "372" meters, and beside it are ancient burials and cemeteries. There are two archaeological sites to the southwest and southeast of it at about 1 km, both rich in their numerous antiquities.

And according to the book: Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed from Qatmah village:

فافرتين: A village in Simaan Mountain, belonging to the central villages sub-district, and Afrin, Aleppo Governorate.

It is a large village located on the southern slope of the Kilsiyah Plateau, in the southern part of Simaan Mountain north of Raqim Mountain and on the left side of Fafirtin Valley which passes south of the village heading towards the southeast. It is 30 km away from Afrin city to the south, surrounded by small fields with clayey soil that is green and fresh. Settlement of the region is ancient, evidenced by the presence of antiquities dating to the Roman and Byzantine eras, including remains of church walls in addition to burials and wells carved into rock, columns, crowns and scattered capitals throughout the village. It is bordered to the north by a mountainous rocky chain and Kabasheen village and Haydara Tower. To the south by a slope, valley and Kafartein village and Qabtan al-Jabal. To the west by a mountainous rocky chain and Suliman Tower farm. To the east by a mountainous rocky chain and valley and Sheikh Aqeel village. The number of its houses is about 100 houses and the village's age is about 400 years.

Its old dwellings are made of clay and wood, and the modern ones are made of stones and reinforced cement. It has electricity network and a modern paved road passing through the center of the village. And an elementary school and a modern mosque. The residents work in dry farming on an area of 160 hectares including "grains," in addition to raising sheep and cattle, and they drink from the well and cisterns and transport from the waters of neighboring villages when needed. It connects to Afrin city via a paved road passing through its center with a length of 7 km.

Village headman: Diab Ahmad

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Fafirtîn

The village of Fafertinê, originally known as Kevnarê, is an ancient settlement located in the Sherawa region of Afrin near Mount Leloun, an area that flourished between the 1st and 7th centuries AD and is considered part of the historical "Forgotten Villages." The village itself contains significant archaeological sites, including what is claimed to be the oldest church in Syria, dating to 372 AD, and numerous ruins feature Kurdish engravings, such as sun symbols. The original settlement pattern began with inhabitants living in some of the nearly 200 local caves, later moving to stone shelters, and finally establishing the current village. Founded by two families from the Azdîn and Quriş tribes, the village was originally entirely Yazidi. According to a village elder, the population was forcibly converted to Islam during the Islamic Conquests, a process also described as having occurred within the last 60-70 years. Before this conversion, the community buried their dead in the nearby village of Basûfan. The name "Fafertinê" is said to have been imposed by a past ruling authority and is derived from the Kurdish words for "stormy wind" (ba firtone), reflecting the local climate. Traditional life included games like "Sêkêlê" and multi-day wedding celebrations that involved the entire community in preparing feasts of germi (meat stew), tirşik, and rice. In modern times, the village also commemorates seven of its members as martyrs, with local administrative communes named in their honor.
