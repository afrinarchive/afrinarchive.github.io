Possible Village Name Meaning of Zaretê

"Çetel Ziyaretê" is the name before Arabization, and means in Turkish "the double shrine", and that is attributed to two shrines existing in it, one of them known by the name of Sheikh Youssef al-Koufi. The Kurds shortened the name and pronounced it "Zaretê".

Source: TirejAfrin Site

From "The Mountains of the Kurds (Afrin Region): a Comprehensive Geographical Study"

Zaretê, Jatal Ziyara, al-Ziyarah, 1906/ - 370 ha - 35 km - 481m:

  • Jatal Ziyara: is the name before Arabization, and it means in Turkish "the twin shrine," and this is due to two shrines that are in it, and one of them is called Sheikh Yusuf al-Kulfi, who is the ancient place of pilgrimage of the village. The Kurdish people have shortened the name (al-Ziyarah) and pronounced it "Zariti."
  • A large village located on the eastern slope of Jabal Lilun. In it, there is a high proportion of people who hold higher university degrees and who work in the state.

جتال زيارة: A village in Lilon Mountain, belonging to the center villages district and Afrin region, Aleppo governorate.

It is a large village located in the Lilon Mountain chain and the dusty region. It is bordered to the north by a slope and valley and agricultural plain and Iben village and Baslahaya and to the south by a mountain chain and rough terrain and Kharabat al-Qanitera and Miyasa and Nabl town and to the east by a slope and valley and Shahba farm and Aleppo Afrin road and Kafin village and to the west by a mountain chain and forestry and Aqiba village. It belongs to the center villages district Afrin Aleppo. The number of its houses reaches 150 houses and its age is 400 years. Its old houses are of stone and wooden and clay roofs and the modern ones are of stone and cement. It has electricity network and drinking water and elementary school and mosque and belongs to Aqiba municipality and an asphalt road connects to Aleppo road and passes through its center to the rest of the neighboring villages until Basuta village. Its inhabitants work in agriculture and especially grain cultivation - and olives and vineyards - and livestock and goats and cattle raising because of the mountainous nature of the surrounding area called Lilon Mountain. Currently modern houses have spread on both sides of the road until the Aleppo - Afrin - A'zaz and al-Ra'i junction and the scattered chalets on the road called Shahba farm located east of the village with a gas station. The village mayor is Zakaria Juma'a al-Hassan and he is one of the prominent social figures in the village and has a position among the village people.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Zaretê

The village of Çetel Ziyarête, located in the Sherawa district on the border between the Afrin and Shahba regions, is historically one of the villages of the Rûbar tribe. Its cultural and economic life has undergone significant shifts; approximately 40 years ago, the community, which had traditionally relied on raising livestock like sheep and cattle and cultivating grains such as wheat and barley, learned the practice of olive cultivation from other people in Afrin and transitioned its livelihood to olive farming. This heritage is now under threat, as for nearly four years, much of the village's original population has been displaced to Shahba. Despite this, deep-rooted traditions persist. The village holds knowledge of traditional medicine, including the practice of bone-setting (cebarî), a skill passed down through generations that originated with an Iraqi man named Hemîd Topê in the nearby village of Aqîbê. Another tradition is the making of "Hebê Salih," a medicinal pill for rheumatism created from local materials like black resin and old wool, a craft passed from mother to daughter. The community also preserves handicrafts (karê destan), with skills like crocheting decorative headscarves (şerbet) and belts (kember) being taught within families, such as from aunt to niece. The region's rich musical legacy is carried on by groups like Koma Çiyayê Lêlûnê, who strive to continue the culture of past masters like Bavê Selah, Cemîl Horo, and Şêxban by formally teaching music to the youth, preserving an art form that was historically practiced out of pure passion rather than for material gain.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Zaretê 2

The village of Şetel Ziyaretê is deeply rooted in long-standing agricultural and cultural traditions of the Afrin region, characterized by its extensive olive cultivation, with an estimated 10,000 to 15,000 trees, a number that was reportedly even greater in the past. Alongside this agricultural backbone, residents preserve traditional knowledge, such as the difficult process of creating a local medicinal remedy known as "Avo Babo." A significant historical shift in local technology is documented in the making of grape molasses, where the traditional method of using a wooden press ("text") has been replaced by more modern techniques. This connection to the land and its products is celebrated in the region's folk music, with songs that praise Afrin and reference specific local places like Sêran, as well as core cultural practices such as the use of henna ("henne") during weddings and celebrations ("dawat").

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Zaretê 3

The village of Çetelziyaretê, located in the Sherawa district of Afrin on the border with the Shahba region, holds a deep and layered history, serving as a traditional gateway to the Kurmênc mountains. According to local elders, the village's original name was Ziyaretê Şêx Yûsif (Shrine of Sheikh Yusuf), but it was renamed "Çetelziyaretê" (Forked Shrine) during the Ottoman era, a period that saw the Turkification of many local place names. The new name reflected the presence of two significant shrines within the village, one dedicated to Sheikh Yusuf and the other to Sheikh Mihemed. The village's origins are traced back thousands of years to its founder, Kol Welî of the Brahîm Paşa Millî tribe, whose descendants are said to include the Bêk, Tinarî, and Sênî[[]] families. A central feature of its history was a 300-year-old Khan, or inn, built by an ancestor named Îsêk, which functioned as a vital resting place for travelers and traders on the route between Kurdistan and Aleppo before modern borders were established. The village has endured significant hardship in recent history; it was bombed by Syrian regime planes, which destroyed 56 homes and forced many residents to flee, and in 2013, ISIS gangs entered the village and destroyed both of its ancient shrines. The village was later liberated on February 4, 2016, by YPG and YPJ forces.
