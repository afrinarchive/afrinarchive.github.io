I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Maseka (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin / Geographic Study:

Maseka (ماسكانلي), Masikan (ماسيكان) /2068N - 166H - 2km - 620m/:

  • Maseka (ماسكا): Name of a Kurdish tribe found in Botan (بوتان) /Lerkh (ليرخ), p. 48/. Sharafkhan (شرفخان) mentions it as "Mask" (ماسك).
  • A medium-sized village located on the southwestern slope of "Bilaliku Mountain" (جبل بلاليكو), bordered to the west by the Aleppo (حلب) - Medan Akbas (ميدان أكبس) railway.

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء' 'Afrin .... Its Rivers and Green Hills':

Maskanli (ماسكانلي): A village in Jabal al-Kurd (جبل الكرد) belonging to Rajo District (ناحية راجو), Afrin Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a large village located on the southeastern slope of a limestone plateau covered with oak forests. It extends eastward and southward over agricultural land with red soil. It is 2km northwest of Rajo Town (بلدة راجو).

To the north, a mountain chain planted with oak trees and several streams and the archaeological bridge (Hashraq (حشرق)) and Firfirik Village (قرية فرفريك). To the south, an agricultural fertile plain and Rajo Town (بلدة راجو) directly. To the east, a valley and mountain chain and the village cemetery and Mamali Village (قرية مامالي). To the west, a high and rough mountain chain and the road (Rajo (راجو) - Medan Ikbas (ميدان إكبس)) and the railway line from Rajo (راجو) to Medan Ikbas (ميدان إكبس) and the first train tunnel and Akash Mountain (جبل عكاش) very high and Surk Village (قرية سورك) and Kharab Majid (خراب مجيد).

It has approximately 100 houses and the village age according to one of the elderly village residents is about 500 years. It is one of the old villages in the region. Its old dwellings are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, and modern cement houses have spread in its north on the adjacent slopes and south. It has an electrical network and the village drinks from a water network connected to Rajo water network (شبكة مياه راجو) and telephone and a mosque. The road between it and Rajo Town (بلدة راجو) is paved in addition to several streets in the village and it belongs to Rajo Municipality (بلدية راجو).

Residents work in rain-fed agriculture (olives, grapes) over 166 hectares and raise sheep and goats. Maskanli Village (قرية ماسكانلي) is considered one of the beautiful villages due to its location and its views of Rajo Town (بلدة راجو) and Akash Mountain (جبل عكاش) which surround it from all directions with oak and forest trees.

Among the village's most important families: (Musa (موسى) - Qaba (قابه) - Qere (قره) - Jabiru (جابرو)) and many holders of university degrees. Among the known cultural figures in the village and at the Syria (سوريا) level are the writer Ja'dan Ja'dan (جعدان جعدان) and the buzuq player Muhammad Bilku (محمد بلكو).

Village Head: Mustafa Yusuf Hassan (مصطفى يوسف حسن)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Maseka

Gundê Maseka, a village in the Rajo district, has a history stretching back approximately 300 years, according to the oral traditions passed down by elders. The village was not always in its current location; it was originally situated about 300 meters away in a place now remembered as "Xirabê Malê" (The Ruins of the House). This original settlement was abandoned after a devastating cholera outbreak forced the inhabitants to flee and re-establish their community at the present site. A significant historical feature of the area is a railway tunnel, part of the Hejaz Railway, constructed between 1912 and 1915 by the Ottomans and Germans. Villagers from Maseka participated in its construction, which involved blasting through the mountain with dynamite, and some of their descendants worked as guards on the line until the train ceased operations in 1950. The village shares key resources with the neighboring village of Mamala, including a cemetery and the historic "Bîra Barjoq" (Barjoq Well). This 15-meter-deep well, whose name originates from the Turkish "Para Çok" (lots of money), was once the center of a marketplace during the Ottoman era and served as the primary water source for both communities. The village also holds deep-rooted folk traditions, centered around two specific sites: the "Kevirê Koxkê" (Cough Stone), where children with coughs were passed through a hole in the rock in a healing ritual, and the adjacent "Darê Mraza" (Wish Tree), where people tie cloths to make wishes. These practices, along with the traditional preparation of fig-based foods like "Bastîq" and "Helwa Kirmonco" for winter provisions, are identified by the villagers as integral parts of their Kurdish cultural heritage.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Maseka 2

According to oral tradition passed down from their ancestors, the village of Maseka, located in the Rajo district, was established in its current location approximately 300 years ago after its inhabitants relocated from a nearby site known as the ruins of Melwer (xirabê Melwer) due to a cholera outbreak. The new village was founded by eleven families: Qaşî Qepê, Mûsê Îsê, Mistê Xedê, Elî Qera, Kenîn, Dordo, Evdî, Şêxê Bilkê, Bekir Cado, and Hecî Şiketko. The villagers' livelihood is deeply rooted in agriculture, primarily the cultivation of olive trees, along with vegetables like watermelon and melon, and grains such as lentils, barley, and wheat. A significant cultural and economic activity is the preservation of figs, which are made into traditional Kurmanji winter provisions like bastiq (fig leather), lêçer, and mûj (fig rolls stuffed with walnuts and seeds), and sold in the markets of Rajo and Afrin. The village also holds unique traditions tied to its landscape, including a healing ritual at the "Kevirê Koxkê" (Cough Rock), where children with coughs were passed through a hole in the rock three times before sunrise, and the "Dara Miraza" (Wish Tree), where locals tie cloths to make wishes. A major historical landmark near the village is a railway tunnel from the line connecting Europe to Syria, which was built by Ottomans and Germans and completed in 1912. Local workers, including the grandfathers of current residents, labored on its construction, and a local story recounts that German leader Hitler was consulted on the project's high cost.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Maseka

The village of Muske (Gundê Mûsakê), located in the Rajo district, has a history of at least 300 years, according to the oral tradition of its Mukhtar, Xalo Ebû Osman, whose father also held the position. The village's foundational identity was uniquely centered on the traditional livelihood of partridge and falcon trapping, a practice known as komirlatî. Ancestors used sophisticated methods, including decoy pairs and large nets (çûl), to capture birds, with prized singing partridges being highly valuable. Over time, the village's economy shifted to agriculture, specifically the cultivation of olive groves, which spurred its growth from a small settlement of 15-18 households to a community of 210. This past contrasts sharply with the present, as many residents have since left, leaving the village largely empty. Despite this hardship, villagers maintain a strong connection to their land and cultural practices, such as preparing their traditional vegetarian dolma with local freekeh and olive oil, and express a firm resolve not to sell their property. The community's contemporary challenges are underscored by the fact that their concrete water tower has never been operational, forcing them to rely on water delivered by tanker trucks.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Maseka 2

Located in the Rajo district of the Afrin region, Gundê Mûsako is a village with a history of over 200 years, which once consisted of approximately 175 houses and a population of around 1200 people. The older homes in the village were traditionally constructed using "kevirê bejî" (wild/mountain stone), and the landscape is marked by landmarks named through oral tradition, such as the high, rocky hill known as "Kevirê Kar" (The Deaf Stone), so-called by elders because its height muffled the sounds from the village below. This deep connection between people and place is evident as former residents identify the specific houses of their relatives and neighbors, preserving the memory of families such as Xelîl, Xelîlê Dawid, Mihemedê Nazmî, Xoşnav, Fewzî, Sebrî, Xelîlê Beşîr, and Hesenê Hesen. This act of remembrance is framed by the profound pain of displacement, described by one elder as "xurbetî" (exile), a difficult state that "burns a person's soul" and fuels a deep, prayerful longing to return to their specific homes, properties, and courtyards.
