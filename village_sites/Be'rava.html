I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

# Be'rava Village (بعرافو)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin)' Geographic Study:

Be'rava, Ali Bazan - Ali Bazanli /719n - 6km - 390m/:

  • Be'rava (بعرافو): It is believed that the origin of its name comes from Ber ava meaning "beside the water," and the village is located on the shoulder of Afrin River (نهر عفرين). It is also the name of a Kurdish tribal group found in southern Kurdistan (كردستان) and in Damascus (دمشق), including "Ahmad al-Barafi" (أحمد البارافي), the Damascus Kurdish fighter known during the French Mandate.
  • A medium-sized village overlooking westward over Meydankê lake (بحيرة ميدانكي). East of the village there is an archaeological site containing large burials and multiple-room dwellings carved in rock. The waters of Meydankê lake have reached its western borders.

According to the book "Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills" (عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء):

Ali Bazan - Be'rafo (علي بازان - بعرافو):

A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد) belonging to Sheran sub-district (ناحية شران), Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo governorate (محافظة حلب), (699 n).

It is a small village located on the western slope of the northeastern part of the mentioned mountain and overlooks westward onto the Afrin River valley (وادي نهر عفرين) in an elevated position above a limestone plateau, covered by clayey limestone soil. It is 4km away from Sheran town in the northwest direction. It is bordered to the north by a slope and course of Afrin River at a distance of meters and Meydankê village (قرية ميدانكي) and Qarqin Small village (قرية قارقين الصغير), to the south by a valley and plain and mountain chain planted with olive and forest trees and Koblik village (قرية كوبه لك), to the east by a slope and deep valley and Qarqin Large village (قرية قارقين كبير) and Jamanli village (قرية جمانلي), and to the west by Afrin River (نهر عفرين) and plain planted with olive trees and Meydankê town (بلدة ميدانكي).

The number of its houses reaches 35 houses and its age is about 500 years. It is one of the old villages adjacent to the river. The region's settlement is very ancient as evidenced by the presence of an archaeological mound west of the village containing large burials and multiple-room dwellings carved in rock. Its old houses are clay-stone with wooden roofs and the modern ones are concrete. Electricity network is available and the village drinks from a water network connected to wells drilled south of Jamanli village (قرية جمانلي). It connects to the sub-district center by a recently paved road and has an elementary school.

The village residents work in olive cultivation and irrigated vegetables on an area of 943 hectares due to its proximity to Afrin River, and they raise sheep and goats. Among its most important families is the Jawish family (عائلة جاويش), who are among the original inhabitants.

Village Mayor: Ali Mohammed Abdo (علي محمد عبدو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

An elder named Bavê Emîn states that the village of Berava was established approximately 300 years ago (04:48). The original name, Berava, means "by the water" (02:19). It was later altered to Ba'rava by ruling authorities who imposed the Arabic letter 'Eyn into the name to assert their cultural dominance (06:01). After the establishment of democratic self-administration, the name was officially restored to Berava (06:44). Historically, before houses were common, many people in the Efrînê region, including the Edibê family in this village, lived in caves (24:50). The village school was established in 1951, but before that, children were taught by religious teachers like Şêx Elî from the village of Qerqîno (07:39). The community historically used a communal stone mortar (delîb) to prepare bulgur (10:07), which was later replaced by a larger, animal-driven millstone press (me'sere) as the village grew (12:59). In more recent history, villagers' lands were confiscated without payment by the Syrian regime for the construction of the Meydanikê dam (16:03). The village was also home to a notable dengbêj (bard) named Silêmanê Çuçelê (38:08). It was also a tradition for women to handcraft items for their daughter's dowry, a practice that has largely faded with the availability of commercial goods (28:47).
