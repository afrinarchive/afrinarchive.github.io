I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

# Gundî Ḧeftêr Village (حفتارو)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' / 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City / Geographical Study:

Gundî Ḧeftêr (gundî ḧeftêr), Haftaru (حفتارو), Al-Dab' (الضبع) /115N - 540M/:

  • Means "hyena" in Kurdish, and it is a nickname of one of its inhabitants, and his descendants still live there.
  • A small village located on the western slope of a limestone elevation.

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها ورواديها الخضراء' 'Afrin.... Its River and Green Valleys':

Haftaru (حفتارو): A farm in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد) belonging to Bilbile (بلبل) district, Afrin City (عفرين) region, Aleppo (حلب) governorate.

It is a small farm located on a plain and on a high hill with olive trees surrounding it from all sides, about 14 km southeast of Bilbile (بلبل) town. It is bordered to the north by fertile plain planted with olive trees and Abudan (عبودان) and Hay Oghli (هاي أوغلي) villages, to the south by a stream valley and very high mountain chain called Sheikh Khorz (شيخ خورز) mountain and Omar Simo (عمر سمو) and Sa'riljik (سعرلجك) villages and Nabi Huri (نبي هوري) castle, to the west by a stream valley planted with olive trees and Sheikh Khorz (شيخ خورز) and Ali Jaru (علي جارو) villages, and to the east a few meters from Abudan (عبودان) village. The number of houses is about 15 and its age is about 35 years. It is modern, its dwellings are stone and cement established in 1975. It consists of one family, the Haftaru (حفتارو) family named after the farm. It has an electricity network, and the road is paved passing near it to Abudan (عبودان) and Sheikh Khorz (شيخ خورز) village. Its residents work in olive and grain cultivation in addition to raising sheep and goats due to the mountainous nature located on the southern side of the village and the pastures available there.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

The transcript discusses the history of the village of Hefter. There are several theories about the origin of its name: one involves a week-long (heftar) pursuit of an escaped family (01:38-02:32); another attributes it to a sheikh named Heftar who founded the village (02:38-03:01); and a third suggests it comes from the phrase "heft dar" or seven trees (08:45-08:50). The village is described as very ancient (03:07). Its original inhabitants came from Şêxurzê Cîrîn, and the first person to settle in the current location was Hûrê Hefter, whose sons Mihemed and Şêxû populated the village (03:48-04:01, 08:54-09:00). Historical events like the Ottoman military conscription ("seferberlik," likely for World War I) are mentioned, as the speaker's father was conscripted (04:40-04:55). Past village leaders included the mukhtar Miliyê Egîoke, Şêxê Hefter, a respected figure named Îbrahîmê Hefter, and a religious scholar named Moradê Hefter (05:39, 05:48, 25:52, 26:01). The speaker's father, Ehmed Haftaro, also known as Hemû, was a famous local strongman about whom a well-known song was written (34:49-35:42). The area was part of an ancient road connecting Mount Kurmênc and Kilisê before modern borders were established, with a historical well called Se'rînc serving as a key rest stop (18:13-18:45, 20:03). During the Armenian Genocide in 1914, many Armenians took refuge in Hefter, where the villagers sheltered them and built them a house (36:14-36:33). The start of the Rojava Revolution in 2012 spurred a local movement to teach the Kurdish language (10:26-10:37).
