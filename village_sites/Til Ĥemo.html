ØªÙ Ø­ÙÙ: A village in the Kurdish Mountain (Ø¬Ø¨Ù Ø§ÙÙØ±Ø¯) belonging to Cindires district, Afrin region, Aleppo governorate.

It is a small village located on the edges of the small Tel Hemo and the eastern Cindires and Jum plain, surrounded by a group of valleys that meet to its south, heading south to feed the Afrin river. It is 8km away from Cindires town to the east and north. It overlooks slopes of the Kurdish Mountain from the west and plateau elevations from the east. Its soil is fertile loamy. It is bordered to the north by fertile agricultural plain and the Afrin-Cindires road and Sheikh Abdul Rahman village, to the south by fertile plain and Al-Freireya village, to the west by fertile agricultural plain and Al-Ramadiya and Hamilik villages, and to the east by fertile plain and elevations planted with olive trees and Tallif and Kafarzit villages. The number of its houses reaches about 30 houses and its age is about 200 years. Its old houses are made of clay and stone with flat wooden roofs, and the modern ones are concrete spread in the village center and around the archaeological Tel Hemo. It has an electricity network, a primary school, an agricultural cooperative association, and a paved road passing through its center heading to Cindires, Afrin City, and Al-Freireya. The village drinks from well water and from rainwater that collects in cisterns in winter. The residents work in rain-fed agriculture on an area of 843 hectares (olives, grains, legumes) and 11 hectares irrigated with well water producing summer vegetables, cotton, and fruit trees such as pomegranates, walnuts, and apricots, in addition to raising sheep and cattle.

B. Summary from Afrin Flo Transcript of Til Ä¤emo

The village of Til Hemo, located in the Jindires sub-district of Afrin, is a site of significant local heritage centered around the Shrine of Abd al-Rahman. According to local oral history shared by a farmer from the Jindi family, the land was once a sea, and a 50-meter deep well used for irrigating crops like okra, watermelon, eggplant, and peppers is believed to draw its water from this ancient source. The shrine itself is a focal point for community traditions, including a belief that a wish will be granted if a small stone sticks to its outer wall. The adjacent cemetery and the shrine's courtyard serve as the final resting place for notable local figures, including Sheikh Muhammad Rabu (1935-1965), Haji Ibrahim Jamil, and Sheikh Abd al-Rahman Muhammad Sheikh Haidar, alongside other community members like Abd al-Rahman Ahmad Mustafa (1928-2017) and Abd al-Rahman Ibu (1928-2016). The shrine is named for the historical companion of the Prophet Muhammad, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, who was born in Mecca in 581 AD, belonged to the Quraysh tribe, and was one of the earliest converts to Islam and one of the Ten Promised Paradise.
