Summaries

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Tirmûşa (English)

According to the book "Mountain of the Kurds" (جبل الكرد) (Afrin City) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (الدكتور محمد عبدو علي):

Tirmûša, Dermishkanli, Dermish (درمش) /2106n - 700m/:

  • Tirmûš (تيرموش): Name of a small type of pine tree, and there are many of them around the village. But some say that the name is composed of merging the words grape and raisin û Mûj Tiri which the village is famous for.
  • A small village located on the elevations of the western section of Khastia Mountain (جبل خاستيا).

According to the book: "Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills" (عفرين .... نهرها وروابيها الخضراء) by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatma village (قرية قطمه):

Dermishkanli (بدرمشكانلي): A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد), belonging to Sheikh al-Hadid sub-district (ناحية شيخ الحديد), Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a large village located in the middle section of the mentioned mountain on the southern slope of a limestone plateau, with clayey soil. Around it are three valleys extending in depth and harsh from all directions except the western edge where there is a slope. It is bordered to the north by deep valley and Magharjiq village (قرية مغارجق), to the south by high mountain chain and Ramadanli village (قرية رمضانلي), to the east by deep valley where spring water flows and Mastur Ashur village (قرية مستور عاشور), and to the west by mountain chain and rugged terrain and Arnda village (قرية أرندة).

The number of its houses reaches about 125 houses and its age is 400 years. Its dwellings are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs and the modern ones are concrete-stone spread to the north, west and along the public road sides. Electricity network and drinking water from tanks dug beside houses and currently from state-owned well are available. It has an elementary school and mosque in the village center. The inhabitants work in olive, vine and fruit tree cultivation such as walnut, apricot and almond. It connects with the sub-district by paved road to the village center and vice versa. It is a beautiful village in terms of location at the highest peak in Afrin City region and neighboring villages.

Village Mayor: Khalil Mustafa Saydo (خليل مصطفى سيدو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Tirmûşa

The village of Tirmîşan, located in the Shiye district of Afrin between the regions of Rajo and Amanos, was founded by four primary families: Fat Kulekî, Bêlêrî, Mûserdî, and Hebîdî. Oral history from elder Apê Hec Fehîm recounts that the village's original location, known as Şemo, was abandoned approximately 500 years ago due to poor land quality, prompting the move to its current site. The settlement was a gradual process, with the Bêlêrî family arriving first, followed by the Mûserdî family from Hec Bilêl village about 400-440 years ago, and the Hebîdî family around 150 years ago. The village's name is believed to derive from an ancient monastery, referred to as Dêr Mûşo or Dêr Mîşe, with local ruins dating back thousands of years to the era of Melikê Tawûs. Historically, the village's livelihood depended on livestock, wood, grapes, and olives, with farming done using animal-drawn plows and a threshing sledge called a "cer-cer," a tool that fell out of use 20-25 years ago. The community relied on several springs, including Kaniya Ziyaretê and Kaniya Qorqê, transporting water by donkey. The village is also home to the Sifrî Dede shrine, a significant site for Kurdish Alevis, where the martyr Şehîd Zinar, who died in 2012, is buried. The long history of the region is embodied by residents like Dayê Elîf, a 120-year-old woman born in 1895, who recalls life under Ottoman and English rule and traditional wedding celebrations with drums and zurna. The modern history of the village also reflects the broader Kurdish experience of displacement, as exemplified by the artist Xelîl Perîşan, who left in the 1980s due to the Ba'athist regime's oppression and later returned to his homeland.

B. Summary from Afrin Flo Transcript of Tirmûşa

The village of Termisho, located in the Afrin region, holds a rich oral history centered around the Milla tribe, as recounted by the elder Xalê Menan, son of Idrîs and grandson of Dilxoş. According to Xalê Menan, who is 87 or 88 years old, the village is primarily known for its olive trees and olive oil production. The history of the village is deeply intertwined with a historical conflict involving the Milla tribe and a figure named Adil. The conflict began when Adil, a member of the Milla tribe, was killed. In response, the Milla tribe, along with allied tribes such as the Rûban, Kêtkan, and Şêxan, gathered to seek retribution. They consulted their holy books—the Injil, the Torah, the Zabur, and the Quran—and decided to attack the village of Termisho, which was inhabited by the Tirkan tribe. The attack resulted in the complete destruction of the village, with the Milla tribe looting all property and leaving no survivors. Following this event, the land was divided among the victorious tribes, with the Milla tribe taking the largest share. The village was later resettled by people from various places, including the family of Xalê Menan, who came from the village of Dêr Sîwan. The video also captures the personal story of Mistê, a man from Istanbul who is visiting his ancestral home in Termisho for the first time in 14 years, highlighting the connection of the diaspora to their village roots.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Tirmûşa

The village of Tirmûşa, located in the Afrin region, is a place of deep-rooted agricultural traditions and recent hardship. This documentation was undertaken at the special request of Adnan Othman, a member of the diaspora from the nearby village of Ma'arasta with family ties to Tirmûşa. The village's history is preserved in its traditional farming tools, which were used in the past ("berê") before modern machinery. These include the dorkê cer cerê, a Y-shaped tool for guiding the threshing board (dêwêr); the şehol, a wooden tool for gathering straw; the heçê for winnowing; and the doskê kefê, a type of sickle for harvesting. Today, the village continues its agricultural life, notably through the cultivation of sumac (simaq), which is harvested, dried, and ground for use in local cuisine like falafel and za'atar, and is economically significant as a product exported to Lebanon and Jordan. The community has recently suffered a major disaster, with an earthquake causing severe destruction to many of its old stone houses. Amidst this, the village maintains its cultural and religious practices, such as visiting the local cemetery to recite the Al-Fatiha prayer for the deceased.
