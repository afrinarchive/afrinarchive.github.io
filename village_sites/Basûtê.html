I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Basûtê (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin)' geographical study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Basûte, باسوطه /4160N - 650H - 9km - 240m/:

  • I believe the word is Kurdish in origin, where Ba = wind, and Sût = sharp or burning, and the location of Basutah village in the plain next to the narrow mountainous strait, exposes it to the cold northern winds in winter, and sometimes causes sharp damage to fruit orchards. As for Khairaldin al-Asadi, he says about the name quoting Father Shelhatʼ: that it is from Aramaic "Fshuta" and means the plateau or shade, and from it the name might mean "house of shade" /Comparative Aleppo Encyclopedia, vol. 2, p. 32/. But I believe this interpretation is not successful.
  • Basutah is a large village, surrounded by Afrin River from the west and south. A spring rises in its center - Basutah Spring with abundance of 15 l/sec. Its modern houses have extended northward and eastward ascending the slope of Leiloun Mountain. It has a small factory for manufacturing some plastic requirements. It is a beautiful tourist village located in the middle of vast fields of fruit tree orchards. Some urban services are available including health center, telephone and municipality, and fuel station. It is one of the known summer resorts in northern Aleppo, and famous for its delicious pomegranates. Basutah has an eventful history, as its castle was a center for the hero Agha Kaj in the early nineteenth century during their rule of Kurdish Mountain sub-districts and northern Aleppo villages.

And according to the book Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed from Qatmah village:

باسوطه: A village in Afrin Valley, belonging to the Central District of Afrin, Aleppo Governorate.

It is a large village located south of Afrin City about 9 km, on flat and fertile agricultural land surrounded by orchards and fruit and pomegranate trees from all directions, and it is flourishing and beautiful. It is bordered to the north by a vast plain and fertile agricultural land planted with pomegranate, apple and almond trees and other things, and Ayn Darah village and the archaeological Tell Ayn Darah. To the south by a rough mountainous chain of Leiloun and Simaan mountains and Birj Abdul Wa village. To the east by a Leiloun and Simaan mountainous chain and Kimar village. To the west by a vast plain and the course of Afrin River valley and Bozikeh Mountain and Kafir village. The number of its houses is about 400 houses, and its age is about 500 years according to the narration of one of the village's elderly inhabitants. Its old houses are stone and clay with wooden roofs, while the modern ones - the village has expanded eastward, westward and southward next to its main road coming from Afrin City towards Ghazawiyah village and Birj Abdalo and Iskan village. It has electricity network and drinking water taken from the water spring flowing from the bottom of Leiloun Mountain, and next to this spring is a beautiful tourist café, and from artesian wells drilled within the houses and nearby orchards. It has elementary and preparatory school and post and telephone center and mosque in the center of the village. A municipality was established in it in 1991 and built in an elevation in the center of the village on the summit of Basutah Castle.

The village municipality serves the village and its affiliated villages such as Birj Abdalo village and Ayn Darah village and Kimar with several paved streets. Afrin River passes a few meters to the west of the village, to cross a narrow strait confined between Leiloun Mountain from the east and Bozkiyah Mountain in the west. A regulatory dam was built on the valley at the end of the narrow strait excavated next to Birj Abdalo village. To the north and west of the village there is a flat fertile and vast plain connecting between Basutah and Ayn Darah and Qarzihil villages, as well as several modern houses on both sides of the road east of the village. Most of its inhabitants work in agriculture such as grain and legume cultivation rainfed, as it is famous for fruit and pomegranate, almond and summer vegetable cultivation and cotton and sugar beet cultivation irrigated from Afrin River and artesian wells drilled within the orchards, in addition to raising sheep and goats and other things. There are several restaurants and tourist cafés, the most important being Ras al-Nabaa next to Basutah Spring. Basutah is considered one of the most important tourist areas in Aleppo governorate.

Among its most important families is the family of hero Agha and currently his grandson Zaki Agha.

باسوطه Spring: A spring in Afrin Valley, belonging to the Central District, Afrin Region, Aleppo Governorate.

The spring waters flow from the bottom of Leiloun Mountain and Simaan Mountain on the northern and western slopes of the two mentioned mountains.

The spring forms a lake at the bottom of the valley then branches into two branches, the first heads north through the orchards and is used for irrigating the orchards, and the second continues its course westward then ends in Afrin River southwest of the village.

The village inhabitants benefit from the spring waters for drinking and daily use and irrigating the orchards around the village since ancient times.

On the northern side, Ras al-Nabaa restaurant was established in Basutah which is visited every Friday and Sunday during spring and summer seasons by thousands of visitors from Aleppo city and its countryside. The spring and its village are considered among the tourist areas in Aleppo governorate.

Village headman: Mohammed Omar / Abu Abdo / Adham Omar

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Basûtê

The village of Basûte is described as a paradise-like location, renowned for its extensive agriculture and abundant fruit trees. Within the village lies a significant historical site, Kela Basûtê (the Castle of Basûte), which is characterized as being ancient and thousands of years old. This deep-rooted history is contrasted with a modern historical condition of displacement, expressed through a song whose lyrics convey the pain and misery of being a refugee (penaber). The song speaks of losing a good life and a profound, sorrowful longing for the homeland (welat), reflecting a contemporary experience of exile and loss connected to the region.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Basûtê 2

The village of Basûtê, located nine kilometers south of Afrin city in the foothills of Mount Lêlûn, is a site of deep historical and cultural significance. Its name is believed to be of Aramaic origin, meaning "House of Goodness," reflecting its natural abundance of water springs and fruit orchards, for which it has been known since the time of its forefathers. The area's history is exceptionally long, with local accounts claiming the presence of Stone Age artifacts, such as flint stone tools, dating back 10,000-12,000 years BCE. Over millennia, the region has been inhabited by a succession of peoples including the Aramaeans, Assyrians, Medes, Persians, Romans, Arabs, Seljuks, and Ayyubids. Central to its history is the Kela Battalê Battîl (Castle of Battal), which was taken during the Ayyubid period (c. 1100-1300 AD). After falling into ruin during a 400-600 year rule by the Mand tribe, the castle was restored in 1600 by the Kurdish Robarî tribe. In 1740, the Ottomans appointed Battal Axa of the Genc tribe as its ruler, and the castle was subsequently named after him. Today, Basûtê is most famous for its pomegranates, a crop tied to ancestral traditions, including the unique method of storing the fruit under straw for winter and the communal practice of making pomegranate molasses (dims) from the sour varieties.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Basûtê

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Basûtê

The village of Basûta is historically renowned for its natural abundance, particularly its trees, fruits, vegetables, and a unique spring whose water is described as being warm in winter and cold in summer, considered a source of blessing and bounty for the local agriculture. This traditional way of life is embodied by a 90-year-old elder named Ali, who recounts a lifetime spent in the village engaged in farming, agriculture, and raising sheep and cattle. A significant part of the village's cultural heritage is preserved through oral traditions like the Dengbêjî song performed by Ali. This lament expresses a deep sense of Kurdish identity and historical struggle, referencing the pain of a divided Kurdistan across Turkey, Iran, Iraq, and Syria. The song honors key figures like Mela Mistefa Barzanî and the resistance of the Pêşmerge against a "cruel enemy" over many decades, while also emphasizing a profound connection to the homeland of Afrin and the importance of preserving the "sweet Kurdish language."

D. Summary from Afrin Zeyton Transcript of Basûtê

The village of Basuta, located 9 km from Afrin city, is described as an ancient and historically significant place. Its most prominent landmark is the famous Basuta Castle, which served as the center of rule for Battal Agha Kanj in the early 19th century over the region known as Jabal al-Akrad (Mountain of the Kurds). The village's identity is deeply tied to its natural abundance; unlike most Afrin villages known for olives, Basuta is celebrated for its diverse fruit orchards, including apricot, apple, peach, and almond trees. It is particularly famous for its pomegranates, with an estimated 20,000 trees of both sweet and sour varieties, and may have been the first village in the region to cultivate them, a fact celebrated in local songs and stories. This agricultural prosperity was historically sustained by its numerous springs, such as the ancient Seri Kaniye, which once irrigated fields of cotton. An elder recalls that in the past, the community would gather to clean the springs, but the introduction of dam water led to a shift where the spring's water was redirected to a reservoir for drinking. This natural beauty also established Basuta as a tourist destination, with a well-known restaurant built by the Seri Kaniye spring 45 years ago that attracted visitors from around the world. The village's long cultural and artistic history is exemplified by the local music group, Koma Raperîn, which was formed by the village youth in 2005 but was largely dispersed after the conflict in Afrin.
