I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Qîbar (English)

Villages of Central District / City of Afrin / Efrîn

Qîbarê عرشقيبار /

===

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' (Afrin) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Qîbar – 'Erşqîbar, Arshqibar, Al-Hawa /6144 inhabitants - 335 hectares - 3km - 360m/:

  • The popular name and pre-Arabization is composed of two words, Arsh: which is the southern section of the village, and Qibar: the northern section of it. This name has been mentioned since the Middle Ages, when someone called Ali Qibar was ruler of "Qibar fortress" located west of the village. As for Qibar or "Qîçar", in Kurdish it means the fruit of an "artichoke plant" / Kurdish dictionary/, and it used to grow densely in the southern plain of the village. The Arabizers initially believed that its name was "Love of the Great" so they used it, then it was called after Arabization "Al-Hawa" believing that its popular pronunciation in the form "Ashqibar" means "Love and Passion!".
  • One of the old, beautiful and large villages in Jumeh plain. At the beginning of the twentieth century it was a center for Yezidi leadership in this region, and still about half of its inhabitants are Yezidi Kurds. It is divided into two sections separated by a valley. Its northern section with the attached plain is called "Qibar", and its southern section with the wide attached plain is called Arshi 'Erşê. There are caves and wide caverns in the southern vicinity of the "Arshi" section at the village's threshing floors. Excavation work near them reveals massive masonry for archaeological buildings indicating ancient habitation predating the Islamic era. The ruins of Qibar fortress also exist west of the village.

And according to the book: Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed from Qatmeh village

Arshqibar: A village in Mount of the Kurds, belonging to the central villages district of Afrin region, Aleppo Governorate.

It is a large village located on the western slope of western Mount Liloun and Mount Samaan, bounded to the north by a plain cultivated with olive trees and a mountain chain and Kafr Mez village and Qareh Taba village, to the south by a wide plain and mountain plateau and Tur Nadeh village and Qarzihel village, to the west by an agricultural plain and Tel Qibar archaeological site and the Aleppo-Afrin road and Jumkah village, and to the east by the northern Mount Samaan chain and western Mount Liloun chain and Mreymen village and Al-Khalidiya and Anab and a very high mountain chain. The number of its houses reaches about 250 houses and the village's age is about 500 years. It is one of the very ancient villages in the region. Its old houses are of stone and clay with wooden roofs, while the modern ones are of stone and reinforced concrete. There is an electricity network and drinking water taken from Kafr Jannah spring. It has an elementary school and telephone and connects with the region center and neighboring villages by paved road. A municipal council was established in the village in 2002. The village residents work in cultivating grains and vegetables and growing olives and fruit trees. Its lands are agriculturally fertile due to the passage of the Kafr Jannah spring stream and artesian wells, and Tel Qibar is archaeological with evidence of the existence of Roman and Byzantine archaeological remains. Among its most important families is the Darwish Shamo family, who were the first to inhabit the village since its establishment, and there is the Al-Namar tourist site west of the village beside the Aleppo-Afrin road.

It is mentioned that the plastic artist Lutfi Jaafar is from the sons of this village.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Qîbar

The village of Qibarê, connected to the center of Afrin, is a site of ancient history and significant cultural heritage, with its origins tracing back to the pre-Roman Kheyaniyan Empire. According to local history, the village's name is derived from the Kheyaniyan king Kîbar, meaning "the great one of the people," or the queen Eşqîbar, whose throne was located south of the village, while the ruins of the king's castle are still visible to the north. Oral tradition states that the current settlement was founded 300-500 years ago by people who gathered from nearby caves and plains. Qibarê is notable for its long-standing peaceful coexistence between its Muslim, Yazidi, and Arab populations. The village is a vital center for the Yazidi faith, hosting several holy sites, including the prominent Çilxane shrine. This cave is a place of pilgrimage where believers light candles, make wishes at the "Dara Mirazan" (Tree of Wishes), and use sacred water dripping from the ceiling for healing. Another important location is the Hecera Şêx Hisên, a shrine with a Roman-era dome where it is believed angels and Melek Tawus descend before sunrise. The village is a focal point for the celebration of Çarşema Sor (Red Wednesday), the Yazidi New Year, which is observed with unique local customs and rituals.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Qîbar 2

In this location, there is a significant spiritual and cultural site known as a "çilxane," a type of cave used for spiritual retreat. This site contains a natural spring ("kanî") whose water is considered sacred by the local community. The water is specifically referred to as "ava miraza," which translates to "water of wishes" or "water of miracles." According to local tradition, this water is believed to possess special properties, bringing good health ("seat xweş e") and having the power to help fulfill the wishes of those who drink it. The practice of offering this water to visitors is a living tradition, serving as an important act of blessing and hospitality.

B. Summary from Afrin Flo Transcript of Qîbar

The history of Qîbar begins with its founder, Sheikh Hanan, who migrated from the Shara region to settle on a local hill, around whom the village grew; his shrine remains there to this day. The village's name itself is derived from the "qebar" plant, a thorny bush that once flourished on the hills. This local identity is set within a landscape of deep antiquity, most notably the thousands-year-old Hittite temple of 0 - Zettelkasten/Ain Dara, which was distinguished by its black basalt lion statues. This ancient heritage has faced modern destruction; the speaker states that the statues were stolen and the temple itself was ultimately destroyed by Turkish airplanes in 2018. Cultural life in the village was defined by traditions such as the daily social gathering of women at the old spring and the sacred Yezidi New Year, Çarşema Sor, celebrated by coloring eggs green, red, and yellow to symbolize life. However, this way of life was catastrophically interrupted in 2018, when the local population was forcibly displaced from their homeland. Their houses, properties, and olive groves were confiscated and, in what the speaker describes as a deliberate policy of demographic change, were given to settlers brought from other parts of Syria like Ghouta. This effort to, in the speaker's view, change the identity of Afrin was accompanied by the destruction of local religious sites, including Yezidi shrines.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Qîbar

The village of Qibarê, located in the Afrin district and comprising approximately three hundred households, was historically known as Samra, a name given by its ancestors before being officially renamed by the government. The origins of some of its prominent families, such as Mala Xano of the Rashwan tribe (Eşîra Reşwan), trace back to a migration from Malatya during the Ottoman era; other founding families of the village include Mala Silo, Mala Miho, Mala Elîkê Hesen, and Mala Seydo. The villagers are followers of the Yazidi faith, and the nearby pilgrimage site of Sheikh Barakat is a significant landmark, situated in a region that also includes the historical sites of Gundê Mezin, Kîmar, Berad, and Kefer Nebo. This cultural and religious identity is visibly embedded in the local architecture, exemplified by the house of resident Abu Nimer (Bavê Nîmir), which he personally constructed and carved in 1976. The building is adorned with stone carvings of the Yazidi religious symbol (the Faravahar), the sun, crosses, and a ship, which are meant to represent the four core elements of earth, water, air, and fire. Economically, the village's livelihood was traditionally based on olive cultivation, but a recent collapse of this industry has resulted in widespread lack of work and a decline in prosperity compared to previous times. Despite this hardship, the broader cultural life of the Afrin region persists, including the tradition of using live music bands with davul and zurna for weddings.
