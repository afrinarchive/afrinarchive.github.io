I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City / Geographic Study:

Ḥilûbiyê (حلوبيّة):

Halubiya (حلوبية): Meaning "gentle and upright" / Kurdish dictionary/. As for the village residents' account, they say: that the village name is composed of two words, Halu (حلو): which is an Arabic word meaning beauty, and Bî (Bî): the name of the willow tree in Kurdish, and there are still large and beautiful willow trees at the archaeological site of Tell Halubiya (تل حلوبيه) located next to the village, and the village and its location became known by them. They are two villages:

Gundî 'Elkê (حلوبي كبير) /553N - 380m /:

The village is also called `Elkê (علكي), which is a diminutive of the name "Ali" (علي), the first person who settled in that place so the village was named after him. No one calls it "Haloubi Large" (حلوبي كبير) currently. It was named "large" not for its size, but for the residence of Jillusi (جلوسي) aghas from the Shakak tribe (عشيرة شكاك) in it.

  • A small village consisting of several houses, located on the southern bank of the valley of "Qara Jurnah" (قه ره جورنه).

According to the book 'عفرين.... نهرها ورواية الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills':

Haloubi Large (حلوبي كبير):

A village in Jabal al-Kurd (جبل الكرد) belonging to Sheran district (ناحية شران), Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب), (361 N)

Located on an elevation and fertile limestone plateau. It is bounded to the north by the Afrin River (نهر عفرين) valley and the lake of the Seventeenth of April Dam (سد السابع عشر من نيسان) directly and the town of Medanki (ميدانكي). To the south by fertile plain and the Medanki-Kafr Jannah-Bulbul road (طريق ميدانكي كفرجنة وبلبل) and the valley of Qara Jarnah spring (نبع قره جرنه) and the village of Haloubi Small (حلوبي صغير). To the east a deep valley and the old Medanki road (طريق ميدانكي القديم) and mountain chain and the village of Kobeh Lik (كوبه لك). To the west valley and the lake of the Seventeenth of April Dam (سد السابع عشر من نيسان) and the new Medanki road (طريق ميدانكي الجديد) and the village of Kamruk (كمروك) and Arab Sheikho (عرب شيخو). Its old houses are stone-clay with wooden roofs and the modern ones are concrete and stone. The number of its houses is 15 houses and the village age is about 200 years. It has an electricity network and drinking water and a temporary elementary school and administrative offices for dam management and the connecting road is paved earth and to the administrative offices is paved. The residents work in grain and olive cultivation and irrigated summer vegetables from the Afrin River (نهر عفرين) and livestock raising. The dam body is located on the northwestern side of the village and is considered one of the most important irrigation projects in the Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين) and Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب) north of Aleppo city (مدينة حلب). The foundation stone for the dam was laid in 1980 and completed in 2004. Among the most important families of the village is the Badri Jillusi (بدري جلوسي) family and Manan Yazi Jillusi (منان يازي جلوسي).

Village mayor: Rifat Hamo (رفعت حمو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

The village, currently known as Sêbira, was historically called Elkê (00:07, 00:48). The name Elkê is very old, said to originate from a man named Elî who first settled or dug in the area (01:06-01:12). An interviewee also mentions an even older name, Hed Gewrîn (00:15). Around 1947-1948, the village was officially registered and given the Arabic name Helûbiya Sexîr ("Little Helûbiya") (01:17-01:22), distinguishing it from the nearby larger village of Helûbiye, subsequently called Helûbiya Kebîr ("Big Helûbiya") (01:24-01:36). The village was renamed Sêbira ("The Three Siblings") after three peshmergas from the village—Ebdo, Welat, and Hêra—were martyred (01:39-01:48, 07:02). This event occurred at Qestel Cindo (01:51-01:56) on February 29, 2016, during a battle with Daesh (06:46-07:00). A fourth martyr from the village, Jînda, fell during the Kobanê resistance (07:10-07:13). Historically, the village was founded by five families, including the Mala Reşîd Cemo, Mala Ceblo, and Mala Horê; later, the family of Hesen Paşeko also settled there (02:06-02:33). In the past, villages had community rooms ("oda") and a headman ("muxtar"); a notable figure named Reşîd Cemo fulfilled a leadership role, organizing community aid (05:44-06:01). The nearby Qerecola Curnê shrine has a long history (09:06). It's associated with Şehîd Hogir, a Yazidi from Endorê village who was a soldier under Caliph Imadeddîn Zengkî and was martyred at the site by Greek soldiers (09:17-10:22). This event is described as being hundreds of years old (10:28). The site has been known as Qerecola Curnê for generations due to a black basin ("curn") there (10:40-10:50), and people historically performed animal sacrifices there on Wednesdays (10:50-11:08). There was an attempt by a state-affiliated individual from Bab to change the shrine's name, but locals resisted (11:25-11:48). The spring at Curnê historically supplied water to Elkê and other nearby villages like Kevir Rûmê, Xerabê Şera, and Gebelkê (11:59-12:13). Other historical features of Sêbira (Elkê) include an olive press that was operational 40 years prior but is now unused (12:31-12:49) and a well known as "Curnê Otoman" (Ottoman Basin/Well) that was a past source of drinking water (13:08-13:12). A bridge and railway line in the area were built by Germans (Elmanya) in a past era, constructed by hand using stones (15:16-15:28). The segment also covers the history of local figure Apê Hêja (Bavê Hêja), born in 1948 (20:57-21:04). He began writing poetry in his youth (22:01-22:08) and created a Kurdish alphabet in 1994 (22:15-22:21). He translated the book "Bûsta Azadî" into Kurdish (23:50-24:01, 28:38) and wrote about the ancient Kurdish Emperor Diyako, who fought Assyrians 600 years before Christ and was imprisoned in Hama (24:59-25:24). Bavê Hêja was acquainted with prominent Kurdish intellectuals like Cegerxwîn, Osman Sebrî, and Seydayê Tîrêj (28:27-28:38).
