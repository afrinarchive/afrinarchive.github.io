I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Gorda (English)

According to the book جبل الكرد|Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Gorda, Gordan (گوردان) /1350n - 380h - 15km - 550m/:

  • According to the 'Kurdish-Kuranic Dictionary' (قاموس كوراني), Gord means the severed sword that does not cut or kill. The 'Kurdistan Dictionary' (قاموس كردستان) says it means: strength and courage, and also refers to marks of certain types of goats. As for Gord as a name for the village, it is a proper name of a woman from "Sêwiya" village (سيويا), who settled in that location and was named after her. The site of her old residence still exists south of Sêwiya village (سيويا), called "Xirabê Gordê" (خربة گوردي).
  • It is surrounded by mountains from the east, north, and west, and there are remains of many ancient antiquities on some surrounding peaks and also at the village site. Those sites have the following names: Bena Ddirêj "the long elevation" (المرتفع الطويل), Pozê Kol "the bare peak" (القمة الجرداء), and Xirabî Kortliq (خرابي كورتليق), which is also an ancient residence site. The current Gordan (گوردان) is a small village located on the western side of the Ole Cce'nim valley "Hell of the Dead" (جهنم الموت), which is a forested and rugged valley where those wanted by the authorities sometimes take refuge. The village is considered one of the areas rich in archaeological finds, and has several caves that were used for residence in ancient times. There is also an old water well near the village.

According to the book: Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed from Qatmeh village:

Kordan (كوردان): A village in the Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Jindires District (جندريس), Afrin (عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (حلب).

It is a small village located at the southern end of the central part of the mentioned mountain mass, where several watercourses descending from the north converge into one course heading south. It overlooks limestone mountain slopes carved by watercourses. It is 15 km north of Jindires. It is bordered to the north by a very deep watercourse and a high steep mountainous chain planted with olive and forest trees and Rotanli village (روطانلي) and Iki Akhur (إيكي آخور), and to the south by a deep watercourse heading toward the Jindires plain and Maska Tahtani village (مسكة تحتاني) and Zindkan (زندكان), and to the west by a watercourse and mountainous heights planted with olive and oak trees and Jawbanli village (جوبانلي), and to the east by a deep watercourse and mountainous heights planted with olive and oak trees and Khaltan Western and Eastern villages (خلطان غربي وشرقي). The number of its houses is 45 houses and its age is about 300 years. Its old houses are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, and the modern ones are concrete extending north and west along the transportation roads. It has electricity network and an elementary school and a Roman water well at the bottom of the village. It is connected to the district by a paved road to the village center. The village drinks from the water of old wells at the bottom of the valley from the northern side of the village and from rainwater collection cisterns. Most of its residents work in growing olives, grains, grapes, figs, and pomegranates through dry farming (380 hectares) and raising sheep and goats. The village's location is mountainous with steep slopes and very rugged terrain. One of its most important families is the Jamil Bahri Kanna family (جميل بحري كنة).

It is mentioned that the traditional folklore artist Abu Salah is from the sons of this village - Kuma Hawar (كوما هاوار).

Village mayor: Mohammed Ahmed Khurshid (محمد أحمد خورشيد)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Gorda

Bavê Selah, born Abdulrahman Omar in 1952 in the village of Gorda in the Jindires district of Afrin, was a pivotal figure in the preservation of Kurdish culture in the Kurd Mountains (Çiyayê Kurmênc) region of Rojava. His artistic roots were exceptionally deep, having grown up in a family of artists; he learned the art of dengbêjî (epic singing) from his mother, Gulîzar Yûsif, whose own father, Îbrahîmê Tirko, was a renowned dengbêj from Kurd-Dagh. At the age of 13, he began learning the tanbur from the master artist Adîk, focusing on epic and folkloric songs. His professional career flourished in 1984 when he joined the group Koma Armanc Adîlan, and he rose to widespread fame after releasing his cassette "Delal" in 1988. Beyond his performance, Bavê Selah was a dedicated researcher who traveled throughout North Kurdistan to document Kurdish history and music, including a specific study on the poet Feqiyê Teyran in Colemêrg. He argued that many regional musical traditions were originally Kurdish but had their history suppressed. Because his work centered on Kurdish culture and history, he was repeatedly imprisoned by the Syrian Ba'ath regime, with his final arrest occurring in 2011 on the accusation of "establishing a party." Remembered by his peers as the "Mandela of the Kurd Mountains" and an "artistic and national dictionary," Bavê Selah passed away on July 14, 2016, and was buried in his home village, leaving a legacy as a vital, yet persecuted, preserver of Kurdish heritage.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Gorda 2

The village of Gorda, located on the Turkish border in Afrin, was a place of significant cultural and natural heritage, centered around an ancient site called "Kebnorê" which featured a plane tree over 100 years old. This tree served as a living archive for the community, where generations of villagers, including lovers and the speaker's own father and uncle, carved their names and stories into its trunk, making it a hub for memory and artistic creation. This sense of close community was also present in other villages like Eynekûraşê, remembered for traditions such as evening storytelling among neighbors. A period of cultural revival began with the revolution in 2011, which saw the establishment of the first formal Kurdish language schools in the Afrin canton. The speaker, Ciwan Hesen, was part of the pioneering group of 15-20 teachers, trained by Şehîd Ezîz Ereb, who opened the very first school in the village of Dêrûqliyê. This era of cultural assertion was tragically cut short by the conflict known as the "Berxwedana Zeytûnê" (Olive Branch Resistance), which resulted in the forced displacement of the population. Now living in the Berxwedan refugee camp, the speaker continues this legacy of cultural resistance by carving a saz from a piece of mulberry wood he carried with him from his village of Gorda, transforming a physical piece of his lost homeland into a "symbol of the resistance" and a vessel for his hope of return.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Gorda 3

In this village, the practice of beekeeping is presented as a significant and deep-rooted cultural tradition. Mamosta Jwan, a local teacher and beekeeper with over twenty-five years of experience, began his relationship with bees as a childhood hobby. He frames this practice as more than a profession, describing it as an ancient legacy (paşmaye) of the Kurdish people native to the region. This connection to the past is emphasized by his characterization of the honeybee itself as an ancient (dêrîn û kevnar) creature. The continuation of this tradition incorporates modern tools, including square-shaped hives which are identified by the specific Kurmanji term mêşdank.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Gorda 4

The village of Gorda, located in the Jindires district of Afrin, possesses a deep and layered history, with local accounts citing the historian Mihemed Emîn Zekî to trace its origins back 10,000 years before Christ to the settlement of the Gutian people (Kûtîyan). This ancient lineage is believed to be reflected in the names of Gorda and surrounding villages like Kimar and Kotana, which begin with 'K' or 'G'. For generations, the core of village life was centered in its approximately 40 caves, which served as complete homes where families lived, cooked, and baked bread in ovens built within the rock. This early settlement was established around key water sources, including the Bîra Gorda well, and this cave-dwelling lifestyle was passed down through oral history from forefathers who lived it. The landscape was later transformed when the original forests were cleared to plant the now-ancient olive groves, many of which are over 200 years old. Traditional agricultural practices, such as using a horse-drawn threshing sledge known as a "jarjar"—a method described as the "work of our forefathers"—have since declined. Despite its remote setting, Gorda has a strong tradition of education; a cave served as a schoolroom (hujre) in the 1940s where children learned Quran, Arabic, and Kurdish, and the village has produced notable figures including the poet Jamil Kenne (b. 1870) and numerous other professionals. Some traditions persist out of necessity and custom, such as the women of the village continuing to bake traditional "nanê sêlê" (saj bread) in the early morning, a practice rooted in a historical, self-sufficient way of life.
