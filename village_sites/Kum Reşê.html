Summaries

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Kum Reşê (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City / Geographic Study:

Kumreşê, كمـرش, الطاقية /695n – 5km - 760m/:

  • كم رش: means "the black cap".
  • A small village located on the western slopes of Atmana (عتمانا) mountain mass. It overlooks Al-Amq (العمق) plain in the west. Its location is a steep mountainous forest slope with rugged nature.

According to the book 'عفرين .... نهرها ورواب يها الخضراء' 'Afrin City .... Its Rivers and Green Hills':

كمرش: A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد) belonging to Rajo (راجو) district, Afrin City (عفرين) region, Aleppo (حلب) governorate.

It is a small village located in the middle section of Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد), on the western slope of a limestone elevation with steep slopes called (Na'na'ah) (نعناعة). It overlooks west on the lands of Al-Amq (العمق) plain, its soil is clayey. It is 7 km away from Rajo (راجو) town in the southwestern direction. It is bordered to the north by a steep and rough slope and deep stream and village Kuri (كري), to the south by a rough mountainous chain planted with oak trees and village Omar Abu Shaghi (عمر أو شاغي), to the west by a very steep slope planted with forest trees such as oak and terebinth and deep valley and village Shadyanli (شاديانلي) in the far west, and to the east at 500m distance village Hassan Kalkawi (حسن كلكاوي). The number of houses is about 35 houses and its age according to the narration of one of the elderly residents is 350 years, and it is one of the old villages in the region. The village is divided into two sections: (southern and northern), separated by a winding stream. Its old dwellings are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs and the modern concrete ones spread in the southern and western directions. It has electricity network and elementary school. The village drinks from cistern water that collects rainwater in winter. The residents work in rainfed agriculture (olives, grains, legumes) on an area of 415 hectares, and irrigation from Black River (النهر الأسود) waters in Qara Dash (قره داش) plain (cotton, vegetables, sunflowers, fruit trees) on a small area of 15 hectares. They also work in raising sheep and goats. It connects with the district center by earthen roads. It has subsidiary farms (Tell Kashur - Surk - Kuri) (تل كشور _ سورك _ كري) and among its most important families are Hasko (حسكو).

Village mayor: Mohammed Hamo Hussein (محمد حمو حسين)

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Kum Reşê

B. Summary from Afrin Flo Transcript of Kum Reşê

The village of Kumraşê, located in the Rajo district, is approximately 300 years old and was originally named 'Kûrê Reş' after a deep, black well in the valley before its name was later changed. The primary livelihood for the villagers is olive farming, and they also raise livestock, including sheep and cattle. The community's historical traditions are tied to its landscape and resources; a central well ("bîra gund") has long served as the water source for both residents and their animals, and a traditional handwashing ritual using an ewer and basin was a common practice for women at the water source fifty years ago. The village's heritage is also evident in its custom of preserving ancient trees, some of which are estimated to be 200-300 years old, and in its cemetery, which contains old graves, including one that is considered a shrine or "turb".

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Kum Reşê

The village of Komreş has a history spanning 200-300 years, with its name potentially deriving from the Kurdish 'Kom Rash' (black gathering) due to the area's dark stones, though a Roman origin is also suggested. The current settlement is a newer location, as the original village was established by ancestors at a different site. The community is composed of several key families: the Hamou and Haske families, who belong to the Rashwan tribe, and the Abdillah family, who are part of the Dimli tribe. In terms of modern history, a school was constructed in the 1950s, which has since fallen into ruin, forcing children to attend school in a neighboring village two kilometers away. The village's water source also changed approximately 30 years ago with the construction of a 30-35 meter deep well, which replaced the traditional spring, although the well's water supply is now reportedly weaker.
