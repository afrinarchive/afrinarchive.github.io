I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Ĥemamê (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' (عفرين) Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (الدكتور محمد عبدو علي):

Ḧemam (الحمام), Al-Hammam (الحمام) /3088n - 588ha - 8km - 170m/:

  • It took its name from the mineral water baths that existed near it.
  • A large village located at the far southwestern end of Afrin region (منطقة عفرين). It overlooks from the west the Amuq plain (سهل العمق). It has an old palace belonging to the region's aghas from Al Kunj family (آل كنج), and the village was a major border crossing point before the closure of the border crossing that passed through it. The village was divided during border demarcation into two halves, then the Turks (الأتراك) moved their part away from the border line, while the border line and paved road pass beside the Syrian village (القرية السورية). One of the village residents dug artesian wells north of the village and built a restaurant, swimming pool and baths utilizing the warm mineral water from the well.

According to the book: 'عفرين' 'Afrin'.... Its River and Green Hills by writer Abdulrahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatmeh village (قرية قطمه):

Omar Agha Qashla (عمر آغا قشلة) / Al-Hammam (الحمام): A village in Kurdish mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Cindires sub-district (ناحية جندرس), Afrin region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo governorate (محافظة حلب).

Located at the southwestern end of the mentioned mountain overlooking west to Amuq plain (سهل العمق) and adjacent to Alexandron district (لواء اسكندرون) on the road leading to it. It is 8km west of Cindires town (بلدة جندرس). It is bordered to the north, east and south by the Turkish border (الحدود التركية) and to the north by valleys and mountainous heights planted with olive trees and forest trees and healing water baths and Marwana Upper village (مروانة فوقاني) and a valley where olive trees rise and the Turkish border (الحدود التركية) directly at 10 meters distance, to the west by the Turkish border (الحدود التركية) directly at 2m distance and the Turkish Amuq plain (سهل العمق التركي) and Reihaniye town (بلدة الريحانية), and to the east by valleys and heights planted with olive trees and Haj Iskandar village (قرية حاج اسكندر) and Ishkan Western (اشكان غربي).

The number of houses is about 150 houses and its age is about 500 years (500عاماً). Its old houses are clay and wood and the modern ones are concrete extending northward and eastward and along the sides of the road connecting it to Cindires town (بلدة جندرس).

It was previously during the French mandate (الانتداب الفرنسي) a center for the sub-district before the annexation of Alexandron district (لواء اسكندرون) to Turkey (تركيا), then the center was moved to Cindires (جندرس) after independence in 1950 (1950). After that it became a small village and today Al-Hammam village (قرية الحمام) is a large village, extending northward and eastward.

There are three old houses on the western side at 15m distance belonging to the Ahmad Kunj Agha family (عائلة أحمد كنج الآغا), and there is an old mosque inside the courtyard of one of the old houses built by the Ahmad Kunj Agha family (عائلة أحمد كنج آغا) and the house is currently destroyed and demolished and the ground floor is inhabited by a poor family.

The village has an electricity network and drinking water from artesian wells and a police station and it is a border point and an elementary and preparatory school and an agricultural association and a tourism destination with healing waters in the northern part of the village called (healing baths) (حمامات الشفاء) and connects with the sub-district and region by paved roads.

Its residents work in olive, grain and legume cultivation where its trees spread over 70% of the cultivated and irrigated areas by pumping from the village springs, and it also produces summer vegetables. The residents work in raising sheep and goats and the village drinks from an old well south of the village and spring water. It is connected to Cindires town (بلدة جندرس) by paved road and it is the last village from the southwestern direction of Afrin region (منطقة عفرين) and Aleppo governorate (محافظة حلب) and Syria (سورية).

Among its most important families is the (Kunj Agha family) (عائلة كنج آغا) of whom only one house remains and the rest remained on their lands on the opposite side of the border with the Turkish lands (الأراضي التركية). This village is considered one of the beautiful villages in Cindires sub-district (ناحية جندرس) and Afrin region (منطقة عفرين).

Village mayor: Hussein Rashid Kanda (حسين رشيد كنده)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Ĥemamê

Originally known as Qişla Omer Axa, the village of Hamamê was founded before 1939 by three Agha brothers—Şêx Mihemed, Erfan, and Silêman—on previously uninhabited land. It quickly grew into a significant local hub with a diverse population that included Armenians, Assyrians, and Turks, featuring a market, a hotel, and serving as an administrative district center during the French Mandate. The village's history is layered, with artifacts like an inscribed stone in the mosque attributed to the Ayyubid ruler al-Malik al-Ashraf, which was traditionally used as a platform for the call to prayer. During the French occupation in the 1930s, local men formed resistance groups to fight the occupiers, who had established a guard post and were responsible for killing seven villagers. The pivotal event in the village's history occurred in 1939 when the Syria-Turkey border was drawn directly through it, splitting the community and its families in two. This division was made permanent in 1958 when the border was mined under the rule of Gamal Abdel Nasser, ending the era of free movement. The village's name, Hamamê, derives from its famous medicinal hot spring, a "water of power" that historically attracted people from as far as Damascus and Aleppo to treat ailments like scabies and skin diseases. After the border cut off access to the original spring, a new well was dug in 1997, rediscovering the 41°C thermal water, whose healing properties for rheumatism and other conditions were later confirmed by scientific analysis in Aleppo.

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Ĥemamê 2

The village of Hemamê, located in the Jindires district 10 kilometers west of the town center, was historically known as Qişla Emer Axa. According to local memory, the village was founded before 1939 by three Agha brothers—Şêx Mihemed, Erfan, and Silêman—who established it in an area that was previously uninhabited and divided the land among themselves. During the French Mandate, the village served as a significant administrative and commercial hub, with the French establishing a district center and a market that attracted people from across the region, including a notable Qurbet (Gypsy) community whose women are said to have dug one of the local wells, Bîra Xatûnê. The village's name, Hemamê, meaning "the bath," derives from a natural hot spring whose water was renowned for its medicinal properties, drawing visitors from as far as Damascus and Homs for treatment of rheumatism and skin ailments. This period of local prominence was disrupted by conflict with the French occupiers; after seven or eight battles in which several villagers were killed, the French established a military outpost (mexfer) on the site of the old market. The most transformative event was the creation of the Turkish-Syrian border in 1939, which physically split the village, separating families and cutting off the Syrian side from its traditional market and the original hot spring, which later dried up. For a time, villagers crossed the new border using special passes rather than passports. In 1997, the medicinal hot spring was rediscovered on the Syrian side when a new well was dug, revealing the same 41°C water. The village's deep history is further attested to by an ancient inscribed stone in its mosque, dated to 488 Hijri (1095 AD), which attributes a construction to the Ayyubid Sultan al-Malik al-Ashraf and was traditionally used by the imam for the call to prayer.
