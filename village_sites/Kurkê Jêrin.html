I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Kurkê Jêrin (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد (عفرين)' 'Mountain of the Kurds (Afrin City)' geographical study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (محمد عبدو علي):

Kurka (كوركان):

The abstract name Kurk means the brooding hen. If pronounced as Kurik, it means small boys or young men whose beards have not yet grown. If its root is Kur, it means the small bare hill or elevation, and this latter is the origin of the name, as the two villages are located on two small hills that were formerly bare of trees. The Arab speakers relied on this and translated the name to "al-Zahirah" (الظاهرة).

  • They are two villages: Kurkan Foqani Kurkê jorin and Kurkan Tahtani Kurkê Jêrin:

Kurkê Jêrin (كوركان تحتاني), الظاهرة التحتانية /932n - 134ha - 14km - 550m/: A small village located on the northern slope of a calcareous elevation.

According to the book: 'عفرين.... نهرها ورواbiها الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills' by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatmeh village (قرية قطمه):

Kurkan Tahtani (كوركان تحتاني): A village in Kurd Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Mabetli District (ناحية معبطلي), Afrin City Region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the central section of the mentioned mountain, on the northern slope of Hamo calcareous mountain (جبل حمو الكلسي). It overlooks the agricultural plains of Katkh plain (سهل كتخ) to the east, to the northwest of Mabetli town (بلدة معبطلي) at a distance of 14km. To the north it is bordered by a slope and stream valley and Donbllali village (قرية دونبلللي), to the south by a mountain elevation and the nearby Sari Oshaghi village (قرية صاري أوشاغي), to the west by a stream valley and mountain elevations planted with olive trees and Ma'mal Oshaghi village (قرية معمل أوشاغي), and to the east by a stream valley and Katkh plains (سهول كتخ) and Kul Ibo village (قرية كل إيبو) and Kurkan Foqani (كوركان فوقاني). It contains approximately 35 houses (بيتاً) and is about 350 years old (عاماً). Its old houses are stone and clay with wooden roofs, and modern cement buildings spread in the south and west. It has an electricity network and a drinking water network connected to an artesian well in the west of the village. It has a primary school and an olive press. It is connected to the district center by a paved mountain road. Its inhabitants cultivate rain-fed crops on an area of 134ha of grains and olive trees, especially on the mountain slopes in the west and north of the village, alongside raising sheep and goats. Among its most important families: Hamo Bazrkah family (عائلة حمو بزركة) - Al Khalil Haji Masto (آل خليل حجي مستو) - Al Shukri Hamid Kilo (آل شكري حميد كيلو).

Village headman: Zamriya Othman (زمريا عثمان)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Kurkê Jêrin

Located in the Ma'bata district of Afrin, the village of Kurka Jêrîn is one of two sister villages whose name, according to oral tradition, originates from the Kurdish word for "blind" ("kor"), referencing two blind brothers who founded them in ancient times. The village is part of a historical nine-village region known as Herêma Kurkanlqê, which also includes the villages of Qopê, Badîno, Dêmîlo, Sariya, Şeytano, and Hebo. The area's history predates its current inhabitants, with the nearby ruins of Dêrmerc identified as a former Armenian village. These Armenian residents were known as blacksmiths who crafted tools like ploughs and sickles before fleeing Turkish oppression in 1915 and migrating to Aleppo; they also built a local mill, Curnê Kolê, which was later used by the Kurdish settlers. The current village was established by several founding families from diverse origins, including the Elîtan and Têlo families from the plains, the Kor Ehmed and Orgitê families from Badîna and North Kurdistan, and the Ebdikê family from the Ne'îmî tribe of Kobanî. An internal conflict later caused the Elî Şêrvîn and Eynê families to disperse to other villages. In its modern history, the village has been deeply affected by the Kurdish freedom movement, honoring two local martyrs, Şehîd Dilşêr and Şehîd Cîhad, by naming its school and commune after them. This connection to heritage is actively preserved through agricultural traditions like fig grafting ("Aşlêkirin") and the formation of a youth theater group dedicated to reviving the Kurdish language and history.

III. Summary from other Channels' Transcripts of Kurkê Jêrin

A. Summary from Halil Sino Transcript of Kurkê Jêrin

The village of Korke Jêr, along with its counterpart Korke Jor, derives its name from an ancient story ("ji tarîxê kevn") about two boys ("koriko"), a history passed down from old ancestors. The village contains several ancient sites, including a 200-year-old bakery known as "Meqbeze Celko," which was historically owned by the Şikrî Heyder and Çewo families and served a previous settlement in the area. The village also has an ancient cemetery adjacent to the shrine ("ziyaret") of Sheikh Hassan, a significant local figure who originated from Korke Jor. Elders recall a past life ("wextê berê") defined by self-sufficiency and greater social value ("qedr û qîmet"), where agricultural work was done with horses, oxen, and sickles before the arrival of tractors. Families produced their own staples like bulgur ("savar"), and this traditional way of life is remembered as having more "bereket" (blessing) than the modern era. This deep connection to the land is also reflected in traditional medicinal knowledge; the area is home to a wild plant called "Zelaton" (or "Tolqoda" in Arabic), a root harvested in the spring that is dried, powdered, and encapsulated as a traditional remedy for cancer, which one local resident has successfully used for five years to treat his own stage 3 illness.
