I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' / Afrin City (عفرين) / Geographical Study:

Xelîlaka (Xelîlaka), خليلاك أوشاغي, الخليل /2104N – 22km - 620m/:

  • From the name "Khalil" (خليل) who was one of its first inhabitants. The official Ottoman name means "sons of Khalilak" (خليلاك).
  • A medium-sized village located on a mountain slope descending toward the south, and it is in two parts: a small upper old section and a new lower section which is larger. It faces Mount Hawar (جبل هاوار) in the south and is separated from it by Ashuneh (عشونة) valley. It is elevated from the north by a rocky limestone mountain peak, and thus surrounds that mountain elevation the three following villages: Ashuneh (عشونة), Zivingeh (زڤنکه), and Xelîlaka (خليلاك).

According to the book 'عفرين.... نهرها ورواباتها الخضراء' 'Afrin City.... Its River and Green Hills':

خليلاك اوشاغي: A village in Mountain of the Kurds (جبل الكرد) belonging to Bilbil (بلبل) district, Afrin City (عفرين) region, Aleppo (حلب) governorate.

It is a large village located on the upper western slope of one of the limestone plateaus in the center of the northern part of the mentioned mountain. It is 19km southwest of Bilbil (بلبل) town. It is surrounded by rich alluvial soils with forests. Water courses descend from it in various directions after passing through it and dividing it into three sections: the northern section, the middle section, and the southeastern section. It is bordered to the north by a high mountain chain planted with forest trees and the village of "Qotanli" (قوطانلي), and to the south by a water valley and a high mountain chain called Mount Hawari (جبل هواري) and Shorba (شوربه) village, and to the west by a water valley and mountain elevation and the villages of Ashuneh (عشونة) and Zivingeh (زفنكه), and to the east by a slope planted with olive trees and the village of "Qory Kol" (قوري كول). The number of its houses reaches 200 houses and its age is about 450 years. Its old houses are stone-clay with flat wooden roofs, and the modern ones are stone-cement extending through the village sections. There is an electricity network, mosque, elementary school, and two modern olive presses available. The village drinks from artesian wells or cisterns where rainwater collects in winter. Most of its inhabitants work in rain-fed agriculture on an area of 200 hectares of olives, grapes, and grains, in addition to raising sheep and goats. It is connected to Bilbil (بلبل) town to the center of the village by a paved road.

Village mayor: Abdul Minan Sido (عبدالمنان سيدو)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript

The village of Xelîlanko has a rich history. Its name is said to originate from an elder named Xelîl, possibly dating back to the time of Îbrahîm Xelîl (2:22-2:46), with the first settler identified as Xelîl Omka (9:21-9:33). The village's name underwent changes: during the Ottoman period, it was known as "Xelîlak" and "Şiyaxî," and later, under the Ba'ath regime, it was called "El-Xelîl" (3:02-3:20). Approximately 200-220 years ago, the inhabitants lived in different locations, including near Çoyê Hawarê (4:19), Sencîn cokê (4:23), Cerrênê Tawîdo, Kelo Şêro (4:27), and a site known as Kola Xelîlaka (9:33). Families, including Mala Bêkirê, Mala Hesuê, Mala Ehmedê, and Mala Kêlê of the Omke tribe, relocated to the current village site for mutual protection (4:35-4:43, 5:36-5:40, 9:38). Later, about a century ago, families from the Keçelo tribe also settled in the village (5:57-5:59). The original settlement at Çoyê Hawarê involved two families living in simple dwellings, with houses built closely for defense against raids (11:58-12:48). Specific families at the old site included Mola Silêmanê Feqî and Mola Ehmedê Hes Kîbî (16:31-16:33). Historical sites near the village include Zêrzemî, described as a very ancient underground structure or spring (13:08-13:39, 14:21-14:53), and Kelê Şîr, a castle said to date back to the Hurrian period (18:18-18:22). The village also has historical ties with Homs (Hemsê), where relatives of some families have lived for over 200 years while maintaining contact (18:22-18:28). Traditional practices highlight the historical way of life. Around 60-80 years ago, medical care involved traveling to healers like Textorê Abo Elo from Koblî or Hesen Efendî in Qestelê, as there were no doctors in the village (19:59-20:24). For ailments like whooping cough, villagers practiced traditional healing rituals at Kevrê Koxkê (a specific rock), a custom also observed by people from neighboring villages, typically performed on Wednesdays before sunrise (20:33-21:20, 22:12-22:23). Traditional bonesetting was also practiced for fractures (24:31-24:54).
