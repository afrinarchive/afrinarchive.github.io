I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site of Kefersefrê (English)

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' (عفرين) Afrin City Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali (محمد عبدو علي):

Kefersefrê (كفرصفرة) /4121N - 1320E - 5km - 400m/:

  • Father Shalhat (الأب شلحت) says that "Safra" (صفرا) in Aramaic means: morning or sparrows, meaning the village of morning or sparrows /Al-Asadi (الأسدي), Vol. 6, p.377/. Perhaps it was something else!
  • It is located on the southern slope of Qazqali Mountain (جبل قازقلي), overlooking south to the olive fields extending to Jindires town (بلدة جندريس). It is divided by a valley into two halves. It is one of the large villages in Jindires District (ناحية جندريس). It contains a large Agha family (عائلة آغوات). Its houses spread on the slopes and it has beautiful villas.

According to the book: Afrin... Her River and Green Hills by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed (عبدالرحمن محمد) from Qatmeh village (قرية قطمه):

Kefer Safra (كفرصفرة): A village in the Kurdish Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Jindires District (ناحية جندريس), Afrin (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a large village located at the end of the southern slope of the Kurdish Mountain mass, 5km from Jindires town (بلدة جندريس), north and west. It overlooks the fertile agricultural lands in the eastern part of the Jindires plain (سهل جندريس) where several steep channels from its north converge to form the Nazam valley (وادي نظام) that cuts through it. It is bordered to the north by mountainous highlands planted with olive and forest trees and Tataranlı village (قرية /تارتارانلي/); to the south it overlooks a wide fertile plain planted with olive and pomegranate trees, Yılan Qorê village (قرية /يلان نقورة/), and Bafıllor village (قرية /بافللور/); to the west by a fertile plain and some mountainous highlands planted with olive and forest trees, Ashkan Gharbi village (قرية /اشكان غربي/), and Marwana Foqani (مروانه فوقاني); and to the east by a fertile plain planted with olive trees, Kamoush Barj village (قرية /كموش برج/), Randkan village (قرية/رندكان/), and Koran (كوران). It has about 450 houses and its age is about 600 years. Its old houses are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, and modern ones are concrete. It has an electricity network and water from an artesian well located in the south. It has several elementary and preparatory schools, a telephone center, and a mosque in the village center. A municipality building was recently established, and it has modern olive mills and several luxurious houses in its center belonging to the Haj Abdo family (عائلة حاج عبدو), Murad Haj Abdo (مراد حاج عبدو), and others. Kefer Safra (كفرصفرة) is considered one of the ancient and large villages in the Afrin Region (منطقة عفرين), Jindires District (ناحية جندريس). Most of its residents work in olive, legume, and rainfed grain cultivation, as well as pomegranates and irrigated vegetables from artesian wells, in addition to raising sheep and goats. There are several modern villas at the village entrance from the south, and the road connecting to the district and region is paved to the village center. Among its most important families (old village residents): the Haj Abdo family (عائلة حاج عبدو), the Haji family (عائلة حاجي) - the Murad family (عائلة مراد), and the first school built in Al-Jom (الجوم) and Jindires (جندريس) districts was currently in Kefer Safra village (قرية كفرصفرة), meaning since 1930.

Among the cultural figures in the village: Professor Hassan Khalil (الاستاذ حسن خليل) \ Tamoz Shamali (تموز شمالي)

II. Summary from Ax û Walat Transcript of Kefersefrê

The village of Kefirsefrê, located in the Jindires district four kilometers from the town of Jindires, holds a rich oral history dating back 300 years. According to local accounts, the village was founded by two Yazidi brothers, Îzîd Xalo and Mem, who were granted a large expanse of uncultivated land by a woman named Xatûn as a reward for defending her property from bandits. This land, stretching from Jindires to an area known as Heyata Tetarê, was then settled and cultivated, primarily with olive trees, forming the basis of the community that grew to 60 households. The area's spiritual significance predates the village itself, marked by the ancient Ziyareta Şêx Mihemed (Sheikh Muhammad Shrine). In modern times, a new landmark was established with the creation of the Goristana Şehîd Seydo (Martyr Seydo Cemetery) after the start of the Rojava Revolution, which now holds the remains of 52 martyrs. The village's economy and identity are deeply rooted in agriculture, particularly its renowned plant nurseries. This industry evolved from traditional methods to modern techniques of grafting and sapling cultivation introduced in the 1990s by an engineer named Ehmed Hacî, leading to the cultivation of olives, pomegranates, loquats, citrus, pistachios, and various ornamental trees. This agricultural life is complemented by enduring cultural traditions, including the preparation of specific foods like "Şorbe Dên" (a yogurt soup known as "Keledoş" in North Kurdistan) and "bizilme kuncî" (a special sesame bread), both of which are considered authentic only when cooked over an open fire. The village's cultural heritage also includes a legacy of local musicians, exemplified by Apê Hemûdê Kilek, an 85-year-old artist who has played the zurna and pîk for 60 years at local celebrations and has even performed internationally in France alongside noted Kurdish singers.

C. Summary from Afrin 366 Transcript of Kefersefrê

The village of Kafr Safra, located approximately five kilometers from the Jandires district, has a history that its residents trace back to 37 AD, an age determined by its local spring. According to an 85-year-old resident, the village is known by two names: one for its goodness, and another as an "oppressor and oppressed village," a term he attributes to the historical departure of its wealthy class. A 1970 census counted Kafr Safra and the neighboring village of Tatara together, recording a population of 1,203 people, consisting of 603 men and 600 women. Today, the village comprises 400 households and features two mosques, one in its upper part and another in its lower part. Kafr Safra holds a strong reputation for its educational achievements, being described as the first village in Syria in this regard, having produced ten university graduates, three of whom are currently studying in Europe.
