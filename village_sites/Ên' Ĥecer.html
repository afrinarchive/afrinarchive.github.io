Basic Information about Ên' Ĥecer

Source: Afrin 366

Etymology: Named after a spring called Kaniya Kêvir (Spring of the Stone) or Eyn el-Hacer, which flowed from a cliff/rock; the village was originally located near this spring.

Springs: Kaniya Kêvir, Eyn Hacere

Hills: Girê Zîkê

Ruins: Delûb, Kafarûm

Other Landmarks: Coyê gundê şêx, Ma'melê Bêrînê

I. Summary from TirejAfrin Site (English) of Ên' Ĥecer

Ain Al-Hajar Eastern

According to the book 'جبل الكرد' 'Mountain of the Kurds' - A Geographic Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Ain Al-Hajar Eastern (عين الحجر شرقي), Ain Al-Hajar Farm (مزرعة عين الحجر)

1 - PhD/Villages/Mala Xelî Pîrê (مالا خلي پيره), Ain Hajar Al-Wusta (عين الحجر الوسطى):

  • The popular Kurdish name meaning: house of Khalil son of the old woman. And "Khalil" is the name of one of its first inhabitants.

And according to the book: Afrin.... Its River and Green Hills by writer Abdul Rahman Mohammed from Qatmah village:

Ain Al-Hajar Eastern (عين الحجر الشرقي): A village in Kurd Mountain (جبل الكرد), belonging to Mabetli District (ناحية المعبطلي), Afrin City region (منطقة عفرين), Aleppo Governorate (محافظة حلب).

It is a small village located in the central part of the mentioned mountain above a limestone plateau, gradually descending north toward Zarafkah Valley and crossed by streams ending in Afrin River valley to the west of Afrin river 1km away. It is 7km from Mabetli town. Its fertile agricultural land extends in the north and south. It is bordered to the north by Zarafkah River valley and fertile plain planted with olive trees and Anbarli and Sheikhutka villages, and to the south by a slope and fertile plain and Afrin-Rajo road and Arjum factories and Juwayq village, and to the west by a slope planted with olive trees and 1 - PhD/Villages/Omo or Sino farm, and to the east by a medium slope and large Ain Hajar village. It has approximately 65 houses and is about 200 years old. Its dwellings are stone and clay with flat wooden roofs, while the modern cement ones expanded to the south, east and west. It has an electricity network and primary school. The farm drinks from a water network that gets its water from Kokan Al-Tahtaniya well network near the public road. It connects to the district center and neighboring villages by paved roads to the village center. Its inhabitants work in rainfed agriculture on an area of 250 hectares (olives, grains and legumes) and they cultivate irrigated crops from artesian wells water in the southern plain with summer vegetables and fruit trees (walnuts and pomegranates), in addition to raising sheep and goats on the plateau slopes suitable for grazing. Among its most important families are Alush Aybash family and Beit Al-Biro (the first to inhabit the village).

From the book Jabal al-Kurd (Afrin) A Geographical Study by Dr. Mohammed Abdo Ali:

Ein al-Hajar /1339 inhabitants - 440 hectares - 8 km - 430 m/:

There are three small villages known by this name, which also have other common local names. All of them are located on an elongated plateau running west to east, and their northern slopes descend steeply towards the Ziravik Valley.

Gundî Omo - Goçera - Mala Sîno, Ein al-Hajar al-Gharbiya (Western Ein al-Hajar):

  • It is known by three Kurdish names: Gundî Omo (Omo's village), a proper name derived from Omar; Mala Sîno (Sîno's house), and Sîno is also a proper name; and Goçera, because its inhabitants belong to the Kurdish "Goçer" tribe, for which it was named.

And from the book: Afrin... Its River and Green Hills by the author Abdurrahman Mohammed from the village of Qatmah:

Ein al-Hajar al-Kabir: A village in Jabal al-Kurd, belonging to the Maabatli subdistrict, Afrin district, Aleppo Governorate.

It is a medium-sized village located in the central part of the aforementioned mountain, on a limestone plateau. Its northern slopes descend towards the Sarfati Valley and its eastern slopes towards the valley of the Afrin River. Its slopes terminate in undulating plains 8 km to the east of the town of Maabatli. It is bordered to the north by a rugged slope, the Ziravke River valley, a fertile agricultural plain, and the villages of Shekhutka and Anbarli; to the south by a slope, a fertile plain called the Ein Dibeh Plain, the village of Jwaiq, and Tall Tawil; to the west by a slope, a mountain highland, and the village of Ein al-Hajar Saghir (Lesser Ein al-Hajar); and to the east by a slope, a plain, the valley of the Afrin River, and the villages of Astaro and Qurt Qulaq al-Kabir.

It has about 55 houses and is approximately 250 years old. Its old dwellings are made of stone and clay with flat, wooden roofs, while the modern ones are concrete and have expanded to the west and east. It is supplied with an electricity network and a water network from the Bir Kukan Tahtani well, located next to the main road. It has a primary school and a modern mosque in the center of the village. It is connected to the subdistrict center by a paved road that reaches the village center.

Its inhabitants work in rain-fed agriculture on an area of 444 hectares (grains, legumes, olives), and in irrigated agriculture, pumping from artesian wells to grow summer vegetables in the southern, western, and northern plains, in addition to raising sheep and goats. One of its most important families is the Amir Sheikhko family (they were the first to inhabit the current village).

II. Summary of Ên' Ĥecer from Afrin 366

Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7eH6HnNe9mo

The village of Eyn Hacere, located in the Efrîn region, is historically defined by its relationship to water and stone. The village derives its name, which translates to "Spring of the Stone", from an ancient water source known locally as Kaniya Kêvir or Eyn el-Hacer. According to village elders, the original settlement was located "li jêr" (down below), directly adjacent to this spring which issued from a zineyr (cliff or rock face). Over time, the residents migrated approximately one to two kilometers away from the water source to establish the current settlement, which is divided into two sections: Eyn Hacere mezin (Big Eyn Hacere) and Eyn Hacere cûcik (Small Eyn Hacere).

The social history of Eyn Hacere begins with the arrival of the Seydê Zêbo family, who are cited by elders as the first lineage to settle the area "berî giya" (before everyone else). Following this founding period, other families such as Elxelyo arrived. The community is tight-knit, with residents describing themselves as "pismamê hevdune" (cousins of one another). Another prominent household mentioned is Mala Arifê Pîrê, also known as the house of Ebû Fadî. Despite the deep roots of these families, the village faces significant demographic challenges; elders lament that many young people have emigrated to places like Heleb (Aleppo) or Tirkî (Turkey), leaving the older generation to tend the land alone.

The village contains specific historical remnants of agricultural life. A notable feature is the Delûb, an ancient stone roller used on a threshing floor. Historically, villagers would bring their wheat to this spot, attach animals to the stone, and drive them in circles to crush the grain. While the Delûb remains as a physical testament to the past, the agricultural present is marked by cefaf (drought), with elders noting that the springs and wells are running dry.

Geographically, Eyn Hacere commands a view of a vast landscape of neighboring settlements. From the village, one can see Stêr, Kûkan, Qestelê, Qerebaba, Sînka, Metîna, and Helkê. The area is also marked by nearby historical sites such as Kafarûm, described as having buildings or ruins where a camp has been established, and the hill known as Girê Zîkê. The village also overlooks the Ma'melê Bêrînê. Culturally, the village maintains a spirit of religious and ethnic pluralism; during the interviews, a distinct and respectful greeting was sent specifically to the Êzîdî community, highlighting the diverse social fabric of the wider region.
